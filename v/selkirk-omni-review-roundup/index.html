The Selkirk Omni, According to 21 Reviewers

The Selkirk Omni is the most-hyped paddle release since the Boomstick — a ~$300 all-court paddle with a removable MOI weight system. This page synthesizes 21 YouTube reviews into one picture: what they agree on, where they conflict, and how much to trust each one (14 of the 21 were sponsored or giveaway-tied). John Kew's lab-grade review is treated as the anchor.

TL;DR

The 21-review consensus

  1. What it is (near-unanimous): a toned-down, more-controllable 'all-court Boomstick' — same stiff/hollow DNA, less raw power, plus a removable MOI tuning-weight system reviewers widely call the best in pickleball. ~$300, USAP-approved, elongated + widebody shapes, Selkirk Infinigrit texture, lifetime warranty.

01 · What the Omni is

The near-unanimous baseline

Across all 21 reviews, the description is remarkably consistent. The Omni is Selkirk's follow-up to the Boomstick — it keeps the same stiff, hollow feel and Gen-4 thermoformed construction, dials the power down a notch, and adds the feature everyone leads with: a removable, movable MOI tuning-weight system (≈7.5g pods that snap onto the edge guard and can be repositioned).

Price

~$300 (the recurring sticking point)

Shapes

elongated and widebody

Texture

Selkirk Infinigrit (USAP-approved)

Weights

~7.5g removable MOI pods, placeable almost anywhere

Warranty

Selkirk lifetime warranty (cited as a real value-add)

Identity

a more-controlled, all-court "Boomstick"

02 · Where reviewers agree

The consensus

Tallying stance across 628 extracted claims, the agreement is clear on everything except price.

  1. The weight system is the star"Probably the best weighting system in pickleball" (Pickleball Studio); the most-requested Boomstick fix, now delivered (John Kew). Removable + repositionable is the genuine innovation.
  2. It's versatileweights on → stiff, poppy, near-power paddle; weights off → softer, more control. Multiple reviewers note it's a rare stiff-and-hollow all-court paddle when most rivals are soft-and-dense.
  3. Less power than the Boomstick, more controlbut still firmly in the "power" category — just toned down, not a low-power paddle (John Kew).
  4. Spin and stability are strong when newelongated spin "excellent," widebody twist-weight "nearly off the chart" (John Kew); ~2100+ RPM new (Pickleball Studio).
  5. Selkirk's warranty/service is best-in-classcited by nearly everyone as the thing that partially justifies the price.

03 · Where they disagree

The disputes

Is $300 worth it? (the real fight)This is the only dimension reviewers are net-negative on. Skeptics: Pickleball Studio says cheaper paddles (Friday Aura ~$120, Six Zero Coral Pro ~$200) deliver similar firepower and he'd buy those instead; John Kew calls $300 "a very competitive price category." Believers: PickleBaller picks it over the Boomstick; Dinking & Driving and JJ Pickleball think the weight system + warranty justify it. The split tracks loosely with sponsorship — see the reliability section.
Durability — the cautionary threadTwo recurring worries. Edgeguards: John Kew's widebody edgeguard came loose while repositioning a weight — and loose edgeguards were the Boomstick's single biggest known issue, so the Omni didn't fully fix it. Infinigrit spin wear: John Kew measured ~16% spin loss after accelerated wear (his "tier 3" durability, better than raw carbon's ~22% but not elite); Pickleball Studio clocked a Boomstick forehand dropping 2148→1769 RPM in ~8 sessions (with the abrasive Lifetime ball). The lifetime warranty is everyone's cited offset.

04 · The anchor: John Kew

Lab-grade, independent

The most reliable review in the set, and the only one with instrumented measurements. John Kew was not flagged sponsored, and his verdict is the most calibrated: the Omni successfully tones down the Boomstick's power without losing its identity and is "a really good, more-controlled paddle" — but he personally still prefers the Boomstick, unconvinced the control gain offsets the lost offense.

  1. MeasuredMOI weights 7.38–7.53g (0.15g variance, unnoticeable); above-average swing/twist weights; QCore controlled-firepower 0.41 elongated / 0.39 widebody (top of "high").
  2. Spinexcellent when new; ~16% direct spin loss after wear (Infinigrit averages 13.8% loss vs 22.3% for raw carbon).
  3. The edgeguard findinghis widebody edgeguard came loose repositioning a weight — the Boomstick's recurring flaw, not fully solved.
  4. Feelstill stiff/hollow with a deeper sound; sweet spot slightly smaller than the Boomstick's "S-tier" one.
  5. His pickprefers the widebody (huge twist weight) even though he plays the elongated Boomstick.
The Omni successfully tones down the Boomstick's power without losing its identity… but I'm not convinced it gains enough control to offset the lost offensive capability.— John Kew

07 · Bottom line

Who should actually buy it

Synthesizing all 21, weighted toward the independent and test-driven voices:

  1. Buy it ifyou loved the Boomstick but wanted less power and more control; you value a fully customizable weight setup; you want Selkirk's lifetime warranty; you want one paddle that can flex stiff-poppy or soft-controlled.
  2. Skip it ifyou're price-sensitive (Friday Aura ~$120 or Six Zero Coral Pro ~$200 deliver similar firepower per Pickleball Studio); you want the absolute longest-lasting spin texture; you want a soft-and-dense all-court feel; you already own and love a Boomstick (John Kew would keep the Boomstick).
  3. The honest readit's a genuinely good paddle that nobody calls bad — the entire debate is whether the weight system, warranty, and refined control justify ~$100 over cheaper paddles with comparable performance. On the unsponsored evidence, that's a real maybe, not the slam-dunk the hype reviews imply.

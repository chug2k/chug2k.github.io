The Gearbox GX2 Power, According to 22 Reviewers

Twenty-two of the most-watched paddle reviewers hit the Gearbox GX2 Power. They agree on what it is — an elite-tier power paddle with a soft, muted feel and a carbon-rib core that should outlast its rivals — and split hard on whether $280 and a fussy control curve are worth it.

TL;DR

The 30-second read

  1. Elite power, soft feel. Nearly every reviewer puts it in the top tier of power paddles — but it's dampened and 'muted,' not fiery, with exceptional dwell time.
  2. The SST 2.0 carbon-rib core is the headline: it physically can't core-crush, so durability is expected to beat polypropylene rivals like the JOOLA Pro 4 (Yola).
  3. Two real disputes: how much it breaks in (none vs. ~5 hours vs. 7–8 days, depending on reviewer) and whether $280 is justified when cheaper paddles match it on performance.
  4. Control is the recurring knock — the muted feel and quiet sound make resets and launch angle hard to dial in. Expect a learning curve.
  5. Anchor pick: Johnkew Pickleball (lab-tested, non-sponsored) calls it elite and possibly his new main, biggest competitor to the Yola Pro 4.

01 · The paddle

What the GX2 Power actually is

Strip out the hot takes and a near-unanimous baseline remains. The GX2 Power is Gearbox's 2025 flagship power paddle, built on the SST 2.0 carbon-rib core (carbon ribs suspended in foam, no plastic honeycomb) with a 3K woven carbon-fiber face, sold in elongated and hybrid shapes at roughly $280.

Price

$279–$280 MSRP; ~$250–$252 with reviewer discount codes; 20% off (~$220) for healthcare/first responders via Gearbox.

Shapes

Elongated (≈16.5 × 7.375 in) and Hybrid (≈16.1–16.2 × 7.75–7.8 in); both 16 mm core, 5.5 in handle, 4.0 in grip, ~8 oz static.

Core

SST 2.0 — carbon-fiber ribs in foam, extending edge-to-edge, with a perimeter foam ring and internal carbon strips. Gearbox claims 4× stronger than honeycomb.

Face

3K woven raw carbon fiber; grittier than past (smooth) Gearbox surfaces, ~2,160–2,260 RPM measured.

Swing weight

Hybrid ≈110–114, Elongated ≈117–120; twist weight ≈6.0–6.7 (hybrid runs higher).

Warranty

One-year warranty + 30-day return ($20 restocking). One Vietnamese reviewer cites a 2-year regional warranty.

02 · The consensus

Where the reviewers agree

Across sponsored and independent voices alike, a consistent picture forms — even the skeptics concede most of these.

  1. It's a top-tier power paddleFrom Johnkew to PickleballCentral to The Paddle Plug, it lands in the elite power conversation alongside the JOOLA Pro 4, Selkirk Boomstick, and Honolulu J2NF.
  2. The feel is soft, muted, and dwellyNearly everyone describes plush, high-dwell, low-vibration contact — the ball "pockets" and slings off rather than firing instantly.
  3. Best Gearbox paddle ever madePickleball Studio ("not even close"), Matt's, Pursuit, JJ, and PickleballCentral all say this is Gearbox's high-water mark.
  4. Spin is good, not eliteDespite a conservatively gritted face, ~2,000–2,260 RPM is enough; several credit a "grab-and-throw" core effect that should outlast grit.
  5. Durability should beat polypropyleneThe carbon-rib core "cannot core-crush by design" (Pickleball Medicine); expected to outlast the Pro 4 and similar Gen-3 cores.
  6. It needs an adjustment periodThe quiet, muted feel removes audio/tactile cues; almost everyone reports a learning curve on launch angle and resets.
  7. The hybrid is the crowd favoriteJohnkew, Matt's, Pickleball Rx, and others prefer the hybrid for hand speed, forgiveness, and a bigger sweet spot; it's a shape rivals don't offer.

03 · The disputes

Where they genuinely disagree

These aren't framing differences — reviewers measured or felt opposite things.

Dispute 1 — Does it break in?The single biggest contradiction. Pickleball Medicine, Matt's, and Pickleball Rx report no meaningful break-in (Matt: steady over two months in heat and humidity). But Pickleball Studio measured a ~5-hour break-in that softened the face and pushed a broken-in hybrid to 59.1 mph serves (vs. 57.2 new), and Pickleball Tech Dude describes a 7–8 day linear power ramp. Several reviewers explicitly worry this echoes the Pro Power Elongated, which got hotter and degraded over time. Unit variance is real: Pickleball Studio's Hyper "felt like a control paddle" while another reviewer's hit hard.
Dispute 2 — Is $280 worth it?The value case is contested. Amir's and Matt's say it's worth every dollar for how it plays. But Pickleball Tech Dude calls the price "a glaringly obvious drawback" and notes power paddles are getting cheaper fast; JJ Pickleball finds it "hard to recommend at that price" since performance doesn't stand out beyond low vibration; and one Pickleball Studio review argues much cheaper all-court paddles match it, so the premium is really just for build quality.
Dispute 3 — Power, or 'sneaky' power?How much it actually hits depends on shape, break-in, and the unit. JJ calls it a top-five hardest-hitting legal paddle; All Drive No Drop measured it his second-most-powerful paddle (pop 37.3), just under the Rhombus Ripple V2. But Pickleball Tech Dude rates power only "decent," below the 11624 Powers and J2NF, and Johnkew's review of the GX2 Hyper shape pegs it at the bottom of the power category — "high all-court," beaten by a broken-in Pro Ultimate.
Dispute 4 — Control: forgiving or fiddly?Pickleball Rx calls control the GX2's clear win over the Pro 4 — a bigger, more forgiving sweet spot that saved chicken-wing defenses. But sponsored Pickleball Studio switched to a JOOLA mid-tournament for better control, and multiple reviewers (All Drive No Drop, JJ, Amir's) report balls launching long and resets sailing until dialed in. The split tracks playstyle: the muted feel rewards muscle memory and punishes feel-players.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball — lab-tested, non-sponsored

Johnkew is the most-trusted voice here: independent (Gearbox sent paddles with no payment or editorial control, only an affiliate code), and the one source pairing on-court testing with measured QC-core and weight numbers plus a guest tester (Eddie). His verdict on the GX2 Power carries the most weight in this roundup.

His measured read: both shapes score elite power (QC core 0.405 elongated / 0.407 hybrid) and play at the top of the category, with sub-120 swing weight and 6.0+ twist weight he calls "optimal." Adding 7 in of tungsten tape per side on the hybrid took it to 8.3 oz / 115 swing / 7.3 twist. He prefers the hybrid; his one gripe is the elongated's narrow ~7.17 in width.

It's the biggest competitor to the Yola Pro 4 — elite power without sacrificing too much control, and a real wow factor. It may become my new main paddle, and it almost certainly outlasts the Pro 4 thanks to the carbon-fiber core.— Johnkew Pickleball
Anchor caveatJohnkew's second video tests the GX2 Hyper shape (a different, lower-power model than the GX2 Power) and ranks it merely at the bottom of the power category — losing a blind skinny-singles battle 20–21 to both the Apes Pulse V and Pro Ultimate. Don't conflate the shapes: his enthusiasm is specifically for the GX2 Power elongated/hybrid.

05 · The independents

Other non-sponsored voices

Eleven of the 22 sources took no payment or giveaway. They skew positive but are the most willing to name flaws.

  1. Pickleball Studio (unsponsored review)Very good elite power paddle, but for aggressive 4.5+ players only; personally prefers the Pro 4 and Boomstick. Flags the ~5-hour break-in that makes resets harder. Stock: 111 swing / 6.42 twist / 7.9 oz.
  2. Pickleball Tech DudeHis current main paddle — "probably the nicest dwell of any paddle right now," high power / low pop, great for defensive-tempo players. Knocks the $280 price and the 7–8 day break-in. Budget alts: Thrive Project Fury, SLK Era, 11624 Alpha Pro Power.
  3. Pickleball Rx (Don & Steve)An equal to the JOOLA Perseus Pro IV, not an upgrade — bigger, more forgiving sweet spot, but stiffer drives with less trampoline. If you own a Pro 4, don't switch.
  4. Amir's PickleballOne month as his main; top 3–4 on the market. "Inflicts fear" with power, but resets and launch angle take real time (he's a self-described 3.0).
  5. Pickle LiWould play best with the GX2 Power among the "big three" (vs. Scorpius Pro 4, Boomstick). Knock: loopier drops/drives that don't dip sharply.
  6. The Paddle Plug (Dane)Rare power-plus-stability mix for aggressive all-court competitors; not for beginners.
  7. JJ PickleballTwo takes — the muted GX2 is "hard to recommend at $269.99" beyond low vibration; the GX2 Power is a top-five hardest-hitter he personally finds almost too hot (prefers the Pro 4's softer feel).
  8. BestPickleballVN (two reviews)Numeric scores 9–9.5 across power, spin, durability; control a notch lower (8.5–9). Heavy emphasis on the RC/SST carbon core's near-limitless lifespan and a 2-year regional warranty.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy the GX2 Power

Weighting the lab-tested and non-sponsored voices most, the picture is consistent.

  1. Buy it ifYou're an aggressive 4.0+ player who wants elite power in a soft, low-vibration package; you value durability and a long-lasting core; you like dwell time over instant pop; or you have tennis elbow and want firepower that's easy on the arm. The hybrid is the safest pick.
  2. Skip it ifYou rely on sound and tactile feedback to control the ball; you want maximum instant pop; you're a beginner still building control; you already own a JOOLA Pro 4 (it's a sidegrade, not an upgrade); or you balk at $280 when cheaper paddles match it on performance.
  3. The honest readThe strongest, least-conflicted reviewers (Johnkew, Pickleball Tech Dude, Pickleball Rx) agree it's genuinely elite and likely more durable than its rivals — with a real learning curve and a contested break-in. It's a great paddle, not an obvious must-buy. Demo first if you can, and don't pay full price.

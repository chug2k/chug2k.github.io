The Spartus P1, According to 22 Reviewers

Twenty-two reviewers picked up the Spartus P1, a $220 foam-core hybrid whose whole pitch is a grit surface called Permagrit that is supposed to outlast raw carbon fiber. They agree on far more than they disagree — but the disagreements (price, edge-guard durability, how much pop it really has) are the ones that decide whether you buy.

TL;DR

The 30-second version

  1. The P1 is a $220 foam-core hybrid (floating EP core, EVA perimeter ring, carbon-fiberglass-carbon face) built much like the Bread & Butter Loco — the one real differentiator is its Permagrit ceramic-hybrid surface.
  2. Near-universal agreement that Permagrit holds spin far longer than raw carbon: Pickleball Studio measured a ~0.68% roughness drop after ~84 games (vs. 13–17% for raw carbon over 100); 4.16% RPM loss past 100 games.
  3. It is heavy for a hybrid — swing weight 116–119, twist weight ~6.8 — which buys class-leading stability and resets but costs hand speed; several reviewers add or move weight.
  4. Power is real but controlled: most place it just below the Loco, well above the Honolulu J2NF, and below the Selkirk Boomstick — high-80s percentile but not the poppiest.
  5. The two open questions: at $220 it is premium, and Pickleball Studio Clips found widespread edge-guard delamination (some new units), advising buyers to wait.

01 · The paddle

What the Spartus P1 actually is

Strip away the marketing and nearly every reviewer describes the same object: a foam hybrid built on a proven recipe, with one genuinely new surface on top. Spartus assembles it in the US and rushed it to market to debut the Permagrit material.

Price

$220 retail; most affiliate codes drop it to ~$198 (Pickleball Studio quotes $200 with code).

Shape

Hybrid only at launch (~16.2 × 7.7 in, 5.3 in handle); elongated and widebody submitted for USAP approval, expected mid-January 2026.

Core

16 mm floating EP foam core with an EVA perimeter ring — the same Boomstick/Loco architecture.

Face

CFC layup (carbon–fiberglass–carbon, Toray T700) topped with Permagrit, a ceramic-hybrid grit — not peel-ply, not infused resin.

Weight

Static ~8.1–8.5 oz; swing weight 116–119; twist weight ~6.8 — heavy for a hybrid.

Legality

USAP approved as of Dec 12, 2025; one-year limited warranty. Not UPA/PPA approved (matters only for PPA qualifiers). The vertical printed lines on the face are purely cosmetic.

02 · Consensus

Where reviewers agree

Across sponsored and independent reviews alike, the same handful of conclusions recur almost verbatim.

  1. Permagrit is the point, and it lastsFrom Johnkew's lab to Tickle My Pickleball's couch, reviewers report little to no grit wear after 15–55+ hours; spin holds where raw carbon fades.
  2. Spin is high, especially on soft shotsIt "grabs" on dinks and brush strokes; multiple reviewers shaped balls and pulled off short banana serves that power paddles usually prevent.
  3. It's a power paddle — but a controlled oneGenuine putaway power that scales with swing speed rather than springing unpredictably; resets and blocks feel calmer than the Loco or Boomstick.
  4. It plays heavyThe high swing weight makes it stable and forgiving on off-center hits, but sluggish in fast hands battles; it moves more like an elongated paddle than a hybrid.
  5. Denser, less hollow feel than the LocoPermagrit dampens the fiberglass "ping," giving a muted, dense contact most reviewers prefer for control.
  6. Built like a LocoConstruction is not novel; nearly everyone names the Bread & Butter Loco as the closest sibling.

03 · Disputes

Where they disagree

The honest splits are about value and durability — and how much you give up for the controlled feel.

Price / value — premium, and not everyone is soldAt $220 the P1 sits above the Loco (~$200, or ~$170 for the hybrid per All Drive No Drop) and far below the Boomstick (~$333). Pickleball Studio, Pickleball Effect, and the sponsored reviews call the grit longevity worth the premium. But All Drive No Drop notes the P1 is the most expensive of its three-paddle comparison by $30–40, and the durability question below complicates the "it lasts longer, so it's worth more" math.
Edge-guard durability — the one real red flagPickleball Studio Clips reports widespread edge-guard delamination across multiple units — including brand-new ones — calling P1 failures more severe than the comparable Boomstick issue (full edge guards ripped off), and explicitly advising buyers to wait. This directly contradicts Ignatowich HQ, who praised the P1's edge guard for staying on (unlike the Honolulu J2CR's). Grit durability and edge-guard durability are separate questions, and the P1 currently looks great on one and shaky on the other.
How much pop does it really have?No consensus on placement. Pickleball Effect ranks it high-80s percentile but below Boomstick and Loco. Matthew K and Pickleball Advocate say it matches or edges the Loco. Pickleball Studio and Pickleball Pursuit peg it near the J2NF (lower). All agree it is less instantly poppy than the Loco — they disagree on whether that's a downgrade or the whole appeal.
Does the feel change as it breaks in?Several reviewers (Tickle My Pickleball, Johnkew) say it starts muted/all-court and breaks in toward a hollower, more powerful paddle over 10–20 hours — Johnkew measured the core moving from 0.382 to 0.400. The open worry, raised by Pickleball Studio Clips and Tickle My Pickleball, is whether it eventually just becomes a Loco — and whether the friction surface degrades in cold or humid conditions (as the Proton NanoTac did).

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball: the lab read

Johnkew Pickleball (with lab manager Erica) is the most-trusted voice here — non-sponsored, and the only reviewer publishing instrumented core, deflection, and spin-durability numbers. His take is the spine of this roundup.

Stock specs

Static 8.2 oz, swing weight 116.6 (high for a hybrid), twist weight 6.8 (massive), balance point 24.2 (high), handle 5.4 in.

Core (QC)

0.382 new — upper end of all-court, like the Apex Pulse V — rising to 0.400 after 10–20 hrs, entering low-power-tier territory (Carbon True Foam Waves, Gearbox GX2 Power).

Deflection

48.9 lbs on the UPA standard (minimum 42) — a soft, powerful face.

Surface

PermaGrit pairs grit roughness with a high, almost felt-like coefficient of friction — "unique," delivering elite-tier spin at baseline and kitchen, and holding kitchen-flick spin better than peel-ply raw carbon.

Caveats

Head-heavy and a touch sluggish; the CFC fiberglass layer gives a "springy" effect with a learning curve coming from pure carbon (he was adjusting from the Boomstick); sweet spot good but below Boomstick/Loco.

A solid, well-developed offering that earns serious attention — it breaks in from an all-court paddle into a genuine power paddle, and PermaGrit appears to maintain spin durability better than traditional raw carbon. A very good paddle, especially for players already comfortable with fiberglass-layered paddles and willing to break it in. (Full verdict pending a dedicated spin-durability study comparing PermaGrit, Infinitgrit, Hex Grit, and Diamond Tough.)— Johnkew Pickleball

05 · Independents

The non-sponsored field

Sixteen of the 22 reviews carry no sponsorship or giveaway. Weighted most heavily, they converge on "controlled power paddle with standout, long-lasting grit."

  1. Pickleball Studio (lab data)~0.68% roughness drop after ~84 games; RPM held 2,044 → 2,017 vs. raw carbon's 5–10% loss in a single session. Mid-tier power (above J2NF, below Loco). Value "very clear." Best long-lasting texture they've tested, with data.
  2. Pickleball Effect (lab data)Permagrit dropped only 1.4 RZ vs. 2.5–4 for raw carbon; 2,470 RPM (DB avg 2,250); high-80s percentile power; sluggish in fast exchanges; $220 premium justified by durability.

  4. All Drive No Drop (3 videos, tournament use)"Underrated"; better touch than Boomstick/J6CR; 2,398 RPM; grit intact after 15–30 hrs; best control/dwell/stability of its comparisons; a potential new main. Comes in light — add weight.
  5. Matthew K (2 videos)Most spin/bite of any next-gen paddle he's played; dense, controllable, "my contender for my main"; co-host Wilson rated the J6CR's kitchen grit slightly higher.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy ifYou want a power paddle that stays predictable, you shape the ball heavily and value spin that won't fade, you like a dense (not hollow) feel, and you can handle — or even want — a heavier, stable paddle for resets and forgiveness.
  2. Skip ifYou need quick hands in fast exchanges, crave maximum instant pop, want the lightest/fastest hybrid, rely on heavy baseline dwell for topspin drives, or are unwilling to risk the reported edge-guard issue at a $220 price.
  3. Honest readThe grit-durability claim is the rare marketing pitch that the lab-tested, non-sponsored reviewers actually back up with numbers — that part is real. The reservations are price ($220 premium) and the edge-guard delamination flagged on multiple units; if you're risk-averse, waiting for a refined batch (and the elongated/widebody shapes) is defensible. The lab anchor, Johnkew, lands where the field does: very good paddle, best for players already comfortable with a heavier fiberglass-layup hybrid willing to break it in.

The 11SIX24 Power Series, According to 22 Reviewers

Twenty-two YouTube reviewers tested the 11SIX24 Power Series — the foam-core, fiberglass-sandwich power paddle that comes in three shapes and (depending on which version) costs $160–$210. They agree on more than they disagree: big sweet spot, controllable power, durable build. Where they split is on raw power, certification, and whether the $200 "Power 2" Hex Grit version is worth twice the price of the $160 original.

TL;DR

The 22-review consensus

  1. Two distinct products share the name. The original Power Series (USAP-certified, ~$160–$170) and the newer "Power 2" with Hex Grit (UPA-only, ~$200–$210). Don't confuse the price or the certification.
  2. Near-universal praise for a large, forgiving sweet spot, "controllable" power, and a plush-but-crisp fiberglass-sandwich (CFC) feel. Almost nobody calls it a nuclear-power paddle — that's by design.
  3. The real headline on the original is price — multiple reviewers peg it ~$100 cheaper than a Joola 3S, Paddletek Bantam, or Carbon Genesis at similar performance.
  4. Durability looks genuinely good. Johnkew measured only 1–2% power gain after 2+ months and no core crush; teardown reviewers found unusually healthy cores. Everyone still hedges on long-term foam data.
  5. Hex Grit (Power 2) is the spin story — independent lab tests show ~70 RPM lost after 100 games vs. 300–400 RPM for raw carbon fiber. The catch: it's too rough for USAP, so it can't play sanctioned amateur events.

01 · What it is

The near-unanimous baseline

Strip away the disputes and almost every reviewer describes the same paddle: a 16mm Gen 3/4 foam-core power paddle with a carbon–fiberglass–carbon (CFC) face, sold in three shapes, deliberately tuned for "controllable power" rather than maximum pop.

Three shapes

Pegasus (widebody), Vapor (hybrid — the consensus favorite), Harache-X / HRA-X (elongated). Vapor measures ~16.2 x 7.75 in.

Core & face

16mm polypropylene honeycomb floating in a single thick ring of closed-cell EVA foam, with CFC layup (carbon / fiberglass / carbon). Edge reinforcement strips on the sides boost twist weight.

Two versions, two prices

Original Power Series ~$160–$170 (USAP certified). "Power 2" with Hex Grit ~$200–$210 (UPA-only, not USAP).

Static weight

~7.85–8.3 oz across the lineup; Vapor swing weight commonly measured 107–116 depending on unit and version.

Warranty

6 months (Power 2 / Hex Grit units).

02 · Where reviewers agree

Consensus across 22 voices

The agreements hold across sponsored and independent reviewers alike, and across the lab-tested ones.

  1. Big, forgiving sweet spotThe most repeated praise. STS scored Vapor sweet spot 9.4/10; Pickleball Effect called twist weight 7.0+ the highest they'd seen on a hybrid; Matt's lab put Vapor twist weight in the 92nd percentile. Wide and stable for a hybrid.
  2. Controllable, not nuclear, power"Low-end power paddle," "controlled power," "safer power" — the recurring framing. Johnkew's measured Firepower is 82–83/100; it sits below banned/delisted paddles like the Yola Mod and below the Joola 3S top end.
  3. Plush-but-crisp feel from the fiberglassReviewers consistently place it between soft springy foam paddles and stiff muted Gen 2 thermoforms. The fiberglass middle layer adds pop and dwell without going harsh.
  4. Strong durability signalsTeardowns (Pickleball Medicine, Pickleball Studio) found unusually healthy cores and no factory core-crush. Johnkew saw only 1–2% power change after 2+ months as a main paddle.
  5. Excellent value for the originalAt ~$160–$170 it is repeatedly called the best value in its class, roughly $100 under comparable Joola/Paddletek/Carbon power paddles.
  6. Linear, easy to adjust toMultiple reviewers note soft hits don't feel exponentially different from hard hits, making it easy to switch onto from a Gen 2 paddle.

03 · Where they disagree

The genuine disputes

Consensus on the baseline does not extend to power, price tier, or where this paddle actually belongs.

Dispute: raw powerSponsored and enthusiast reviewers lean generous — Matt's Pickleball scored the series 8.9/10 and called power "comparable to the Yola 3S"; STS gave power 9.1/10. Independents are cooler: Pickleball Studio called Power 2 "mid-tier, even low-tier without added weight," Pickleball Effect labeled the Vapor a "low-end power paddle," and Martin Grunin found "less outright power on drives than the Inferno, Boomstick, or Cyclone even after 6g of lead." The honest read: above-average pop, mid-pack drive power that wants lead tape.
Dispute: price and value tierTwo paddles, two value stories. The $160–$170 original is near-unanimously a value standout (~$100 under rivals). The ~$200–$210 Power 2 is contested: STS frames it as a $133 saving vs. the Selkirk Boomstick ($333); Pickleball Studio (twice) says the value case is unproven until Hex Grit durability is established — "if it wears down to raw carbon levels it is not special." Pickleball Tech Dude calls it "the best value in pickleball at ~$190" anyway, citing QC consistency.
Dispute: certification — and which paddle you're buyingThis is the sharpest split, and it tracks the version. The original Power Series is USAP / PB Core 43 certified (legal for sanctioned amateur play). The Hex Grit Power 2 is UPA-only — too rough to pass USAP's roughness test — so it's barred from USAP A-tour, US Open, and Nationals. STS calls UPA-only "one of the smartest moves" (RPM-limited, lets elite grit stay pro-legal); Pickleball Studio and Matt's flag it as a real disqualifier for tournament players.
Dispute: which shape, and a real weaknessThe Vapor is the consensus pick, but the elongated Harache-X drew the only strongly negative review. Martin Grunin will NOT main it: he found a "distinct drop-off in pop and power at the top of the paddle face" and significant twisting on high contact — fatal for his counter-heavy game. Pickleball Tech Dude independently flagged a "dead zone above the top corners" as a solidity issue. Hit mid-face and it's great; hit high and it punishes you.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball — the most-trusted, lab-tested voice

Johnkew Pickleball is the reliability anchor here: measured Firepower scores, QR rebound numbers, and an accelerated ball-cannon spin-durability test. Both of his reviews are sponsored — flagged — but his claims are the ones backed by instruments, so they carry the most weight.

Firepower 82/100

Just under the Joola 3S and Paddletek Bantam TKO CX; in the same ballpark as the Carbon True Foam and forthcoming Franklin C45.

Durability measured

Control above its class

05 · Other independent voices

The non-sponsored corroboration

Twelve of the 22 reviews are non-sponsored, including two other lab-equipped channels. They broadly confirm Johnkew while being franker about the soft spots.

  1. Pickleball Studio (4 reviews, independent)Put the original Vapor in their personal top-3 tournament paddles and used it at a tournament in Hawaii. On Hex Grit Power 2: spin dropped only ~70 RPM (2141→2071) after 100 games vs. raw carbon's 300–400 RPM fall; roughness down just 4.39% vs. ~20%. But power is "mid-to-low tier" stock and soft-game dialing was occasionally inconsistent — top 5, not an immediate main.
  2. Matt's Pickleball (4 reviews, independent + lab)Measured Hex Grit Vapor at 2,335 RPM (elite in a 440+ paddle database), 61.1 mph serve (top tier), swing weight just 107.55. Highest absolute roughness retained after 50 hours vs. three rivals (12% loss). Caveat: UPA-only, wants lead tape, not max out-of-box forgiveness.
  3. Pickleball Effect (2 reviews, independent)"I just don't know how you don't buy this." Elite spin (~2,100 RPM, top ~10 ever measured), favorable swing/twist ratio, $199. Calls the original a "low-end power paddle" that transitions well from control play; less raw pop than a Yola 3S or Bantam.
  4. Pickleball Tech Dude (independent)"Best value in pickleball at ~$190." Hex Grit ~9–9.5 Mohs; >95% spin retention after 50–60 games across six tested units with zero QC issues. Knocks it for a top-corner dead zone and below-average raw power.
  5. Pickleball Pursuit / Louis (independent)"Legitimately elite" spin; power in line with Spartis P1, below the Bread & Butter Loco. Easy recommend if Hex Grit holds up over 3–4 months.
  6. Martin Grunin (independent)The dissent — won't main the elongated Harache-X due to top-of-face pop drop-off, though he praises spin, serve confidence, and grit longevity (~2% loss at 90 hours per the brand).
  7. PickleballPlaybook / Austin Hardy (independent)One of his all-time favorites; spin "insane" and consistent across the face. Wants a touch more counter-attack pop.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy the ORIGINAL Power Series (~$160–$170) ifYou want a forgiving, controllable power paddle that is USAP-legal for sanctioned amateur play, and you want to save ~$100 over a Joola 3S / Paddletek Bantam / Carbon Genesis. The Vapor (hybrid) is the consensus best shape.
  2. Buy the Power 2 / Hex Grit (~$200–$210) ifDurable elite spin is your priority and you don't need USAP certification (rec play, PPA/UPA events). Lab tests back the grit-longevity claim (~70 RPM lost over 100 games vs. 300–400 for raw carbon). Plan to add a few grams of lead tape for power and stability.
  3. Skip it ifYou play USAP-sanctioned tournaments and want the Hex Grit version (it's barred); you want top-tier raw drive power out of the box (look at the Loco, Boomstick, Inferno); you naturally hit high on the face and are eyeing the elongated Harache-X (top-corner drop-off); or you're a beginner who wants a pure control paddle.
  4. The honest readTwo paddles wearing one name. The cheap one is a near-unanimous value pick with real power-paddle control. The expensive one is a spin specialist whose whole case rests on Hex Grit durability — and unusually, the independent lab data so far supports it. Mid-pack raw power and (on Power 2) lost USAP certification are the consistent costs.

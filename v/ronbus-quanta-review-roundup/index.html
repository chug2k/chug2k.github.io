The Ronbus Quanta, According to 22 Reviewers

A $99 full-foam Gen 4 power paddle that nearly every reviewer loves — but only after you bolt 15 grams of lead onto it. We read 22 reviews to separate the consensus from the spin.

Creator: clpanicPublished: 2026-06-05 18:17:29+00:00
Watch on YouTube
TL;DR

The 30-second version

  1. $99–$100 with a discount code (≈$120 MSRP) for a full-foam Gen 4 paddle — every reviewer calls the value exceptional; many say it'd sell at double the price.
  2. Out of the box it's mediocre: ultra-low swing weight (≈101–111), low twist weight, small sweet spot, twists in the hand. Nearly all 22 reviewers say you MUST add ~15g of perimeter weight.
  3. Weighted up, it lands in the upper-mid power tier — roughly 80–95% of a Selkirk Boomstick ($333) at about a third the price. Boomstick still edges it on peak pop and spin.
  4. Five shapes (R1–R5). R3 and R4 are the most-recommended; R5 drew a few complaints (mishits). Spin ratings split hard — labs say 74th–94th percentile, several feel reviewers say it's weak on soft touch.
  5. Lab anchor Johnkew Pickleball ranks the weighted Quanta closest to the Bread & Butter Loco — above mid power, below the Boomstick / GX2 Power / Yola Pro 4. Buy it if you tinker; skip stock if you want plug-and-play.

01 · The paddle

What the Ronbus Quanta is

Near-unanimous baseline across all 22 reviews: the Quanta is a full-foam, Gen 4, thermoformed power paddle sold in five shapes for about a hundred dollars. The construction details line up source to source — an expanded-polymer (EP) foam center, an EVA foam ring/horseshoe around the hitting zone for the trampoline effect, and TPE (thermoplastic elastomer) inserts in the bottom corners for vibration damping. No polypropylene honeycomb anywhere, including the handle.

Price

≈$120 MSRP, $99–$100 with a creator discount code (one Canadian shop: $170 CAD).

Construction

Full-foam Gen 4 — EP core, EVA ring, TPE corner inserts. Foam runs into the handle.

Face & frame

Raw T700 carbon fiber, thermoformed, carbon-grid edge lattice; 16mm core throughout.

Five shapes

R1 (elongated, curved/arrow top), R2 (widebody), R3 (elongated, square top), R4 (hybrid), R5 (flared widebody).

Static weight

≈7.6–7.9 oz — well under the ~8 oz market average.

Swing weight

≈101 (R2 widebody) to ≈110–112 (R3 elongated) — exceptionally low for the category (most power paddles 115–125).

Twist weight

Low, roughly 5.5–6.4 stock — the root of the stock stability complaints.

Handle

5.5", ~4.25" grip, hard PU pallets — a near-universal "big improvement over the old Ripple" note.

Certification / warranty

USAP tournament-legal; warranty reported as 6 months to 1 year (a knock vs. Boomstick's lifetime).

02 · Consensus

What basically everyone agrees on

Strip away the price and the disagreements and a remarkably consistent story remains. The single loudest point of agreement: this paddle is a project, not a plug-and-play.

  1. It must be weighted~18 of 22 reviewers added perimeter weight, almost always ~15g total (7.5g per side at 3 & 9 o'clock, Boomstick-style). The Pickleball Dad and JJ landed near 8g/side for ~112 swing / ~8.0 twist.
  2. Stock = underwhelmingRepeated verbatim themes — "very mid," "feels like a toy," "unplayable" stock, twists in the hand on off-center hits, small sweet spot, high launch angle.
  3. Weighted = transformedAdding mass deepens the sound (toward Boomstick "thud"), enlarges the sweet spot dramatically, kills the twisting, and unlocks real power and plow-through.
  4. Value is the headline"Best value paddle on the market," "highest value-to-performance ratio I've ever hit," "you could buy three Quantas for one Boomstick." Universal.
  5. The handle is fixedHard pallets replace the Ripple's squishy ones — called among the best handles in the industry by multiple reviewers (one minor gripe: sharp corners).
  6. It beats the Ronbus RippleEvery reviewer who compared them prefers the Quanta over the Ripple V2 — lighter, cheaper, more customizable, "everything the Ripple should have been."
  7. The lineup is confusingSeveral flag the odd strategy — best paddle at the lowest price, launched days after the $180 Reoam and months after the Ripple V2.

03 · The disputes

Where reviewers genuinely disagree

This is where a roundup earns its keep. The headline number — value — is consensus; almost everything about performance is contested.

Dispute 1 — How close to the Boomstick, really?The single biggest split. Pickleball Studio's data put the weighted Quanta at 58.3 mph serves vs. Boomstick 57.6 (essentially identical). Matt's Pickleball and the unnamed Studio hosts say the gap is ~10–15% / "barely tell them apart in a blind sound test." Pickleball Tech Dude (Rafa) is more conservative: ~80–85% of the Boomstick. West Side Pickleball, after a full singles match, is blunt — the Boomstick won the game and its sweet spot is "way way way better, not even close." So: near-parity to clearly-behind, depending on who's swinging.
Dispute 2 — Spin: elite or weak?The lab and feel camps openly contradict each other. Matt's Pickleball measured 2265–2316 RPM (74th–94th percentile) and calls spin "excellent." All Drive No Drop got 2145–2232 RPM ("getting into elite"). But Rackets & Runners and West Side Pickleball say the smooth-ish face is weak on soft/touch spin — "gave up trying to rely on soft spin," "very limited dinking spin," compared unfavorably to a textured Ruby Pro. Reconcile: strong spin on full swings, mediocre on touch.
Dispute 3 — Control & feel characterIs it muted or poppy? PickleballPlugOC made it his main paddle specifically because it plays "muted, not poppy" with great reset control. The Pickleball Dad calls it "very hollow," loud, and hard to control at the net ("barely needs to be touched to pop over"). Rackets & Runners (Luca) calls stock pocketing inconsistent — "randomly firms up." Same paddle, opposite descriptions — heavily weight- and shape-dependent.
Dispute 4 — Durability & warrantyGenuinely unresolved. Pickleball Medicine calls it "free of risk of core crushing" and praises the build; Pickleball Studio approves the harder pallets. But most reviewers explicitly say durability is unknown ("very early impressions, we'll track it"). West Side Pickleball flags the warranty as only 6 months (below their 1-year minimum); the Quanta's warranty is a recurring asterisk against the Boomstick's lifetime guarantee.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball (lab-tested, not sponsored)

Weighted heaviest here: Johnkew Pickleball (John and Eddie) is the reliability anchor — measurement-driven and not sponsored. Their placement is the most carefully calibrated of the group.

Swing weight measured

~101 widebody to ~110 on the R3 elongated — "the lowest swing weight I've measured on an elongated paddle," at a full 16mm thickness ("crazy low").

Weighting

~7.5g per side at 3 & 9 — stock plays generic/poppy with too-high launch and not enough mass to stop twist.

Power placement

Weighted, it sits at the upper end of the power scale but not elite — ABOVE the Bread & Butter Loco, BELOW the Selkirk Boomstick, Gearbox GX2 Power, and Yola Pro 4.

Closest comp

The Loco — medium-stiffness, dwell-heavy, pockets the ball more than the Boomstick.

Sound

Deeper, bassier, more muted than the Boomstick's loud crack — they call it more pleasing.

Spin

Slightly below the Boomstick, but "not enough to justify the $233 price gap for most players."

It would be a hot seller at double the price. The gap between this and the $333 Boomstick does not justify the price difference for most players.— Johnkew Pickleball (John & Eddie)

05 · Independent voices

Other non-sponsored reviewers

Sixteen of the 22 reviews are non-sponsored. Their consensus mirrors Johnkew's; the disagreements are mostly about how demanding the paddle is.

  1. Pickleball Studio (unsponsored first-impressions)"Everything the Ripple wanted to be." Weighted, nearly indistinguishable from the Boomstick in a blind bounce test; zero complaints after weighting. Estimates Boomstick's pop edge at ~10–15%.
  2. Pickleball Tech Dude / RafaHighest value-to-performance ratio he's ever hit; weighted, ~80–85% of the Loco and Boomstick at half / a quarter the price. Only the R1 and R3 shapes earn a genuine recommendation; flags weak twist weight.
  3. Matt's PickleballLab numbers — R4 hit 54.1 mph serves (effectively tied 1st) and 30.9 mph punch volleys (2nd behind the Loco Hybrid). Serve/punch speeds 58.5–59.4 / 38.4–38.7 mph land 75th–94th percentile. "Budget paddle of the year territory." R3/R4 best balanced.
  4. All Drive No DropR4 hybrid and R2 widebody became the leaders in his power-per-swing-weight database, beating the long-standing Pallettech ESQC. Stock R4 measured 38.4 pop ("almost up there with the Boomsticks"). R4 his favorite.
  5. PickleballPlugOCMade it his MAIN paddle — control, resets, and build quality beat the Boomstick; loses on raw power. ~$105 shipped vs. $333+.
  6. JJ PickleballRonbus's best paddle once weighted; better control than the Boomstick at under a third the price — but peak power lands in the lower half of power paddles, and the loud sound "gives an illusion of more power than it delivers."
  7. West Side Pickleball (bought with own money)Scored R3 stock 32/60, weighted 37/60 ("above average, pretty darn good for $100"). Boomstick better in every category; Quanta wins on value. A second West Side review reached the same verdict.
  8. Rackets & Runners / Luca (unsponsored)Most powerful of three compared, but least balanced — Ronbus "may have gone too far chasing power" at the cost of control. Needs heavy lead to smooth out randomness.
  9. Pickleball RxWeighted, hits "like a truck," near Boomstick power. Best for 3.0–3.5 players moving up from control paddles; explicitly NOT for beginners. Loses to the Luz Cannon on ease of use.
  10. The Pickleball Dad"So much fun," a head-turner; huge sweet spot, predictable power once dialed (took ~2 weeks). Settled on 8g at 3 & 9 → ~112 swing / 8.03 twist.
  11. Hometown Pickleball (2 model-guide videos)Sells the paddle but the second video isn't a sponsored review — a shape guide; R1 his top pick, then R4, then R5; says weighting is "not as difficult" as others claim.

07 · The verdict

Who should buy it

  1. Buy it ifYou tinker — you own a scale and lead/tungsten tape, want ~85–95% of a $300 power paddle for $100, and like a low-swing-weight base you can tune. R3 or R4 are the safest shape picks; R2/R5 for the largest sweet spot and fastest hands.
  2. Buy it if (also)You want a forgiving on-ramp to power-paddle play, a junior/low-arm-strain player who needs ultra-light, or you value control-oriented resets (PickleballPlugOC made it his main on exactly that).
  3. Skip it ifYou want plug-and-play out of the box (it plays "mid" to "unplayable" stock), you rely on a soft-touch spin game, you're a true beginner, or you already own a Boomstick / Loco / J2NF / Flick F1 — multiple reviewers call it redundant in that case.
  4. Honest readA genuinely great $100 paddle with a $20 tax in tape and a couple weeks of adjustment. It does not beat the Boomstick on peak power, spin, or stock playability — it beats it on price by ~$233, and for most players that math wins. Buy three, the lifetime-cost argument still favors the Quanta.

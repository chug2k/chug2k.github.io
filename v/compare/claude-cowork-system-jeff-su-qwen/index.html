Build a Personal "Claude OS" — Your Own AI Co-worker Without Burning Rate Limits

A simple system of markdown files can turn Claude into a personal co-worker that remembers your context, follows your rules, and handles multi-step tasks across email, finances, and more — without hitting rate limits.

01 · The hook

A hung-over AI that still gets things done

The video opens with a skit: the creator claims to be hung over and hands off a to-do list — finalize a workshop outline, write a newsletter, review expenses. The AI co-worker (Co-work) completes it all in seconds, drafting copy, pulling spending data, and flagging a questionable Bumble Premium charge. The creator's punchline: "All right, not bad. It's not actually a skit — the demo is real. Co-work is a folder-based system of markdown files that tells Claude how to behave, remember, and write in your voice.

02 · The hierarchy

Three levels of rules, like a constitution

Co-work uses a 3-level hierarchy modeled on the US Constitution:

  • Level 0 — Root (claude.md): The "Constitution." Applies to every task. Contains memory system rules, a routing map, and references to resource files.
  • Level 1 — Workstations: Subfolders (e.g., Email HQ, Personal Finances) with their own claude.md, memory.md, and resources/ — adding domain-specific rules on top of the root.
  • Level 2 — Projects: Individual project folders inside workstations for one-off work (a workshop, a trip, a mortgage refinance).
claude.md is the instruction manual that tells Co-work how to behave.— Jeff

03 · Root setup

memory, routing, and voice

Every Co-work workspace starts with three files:

  1. claude.md — Core rules: memory system (read memory.md every session; write to it on "remember this"), routing map (which workstation to load for which task), and references to resource files.
  2. memory.md — A notepad. Stores active projects, recent memories, and progress. Claude auto-adds entries when prompted.
  3. resources/voice-principles.md — Writing patterns extracted from your past emails or writing samples (greeting style, sign-offs, tone rules).

Voice principles are extracted automatically: paste a prompt template into a new session (optionally with Gmail connected to analyze 30 sent emails), and Co-work builds your writing profile — warm, direct, professional, leads with transparency, no corporate jargon.

04 · Workstations

Universal and dedicated areas of your life

Workstations are where the system scales. There are two types:

Universal workstations handle cross-cutting concerns: - Email HQ — Analyzes 4 weeks of sent emails to extract greeting, sign-off, and threading conventions. Stacks on top of root voice principles with email-specific rules (inbox zero workflow, 2-minute rule, label strategy).

Dedicated workstations handle one life area: - Personal Finances — Import 12 months of credit card statements; Co-work builds a spending tracker spreadsheet with categories, monthly/ yearly summaries, and a taxonomy you can correct and that persists.

Both types are created from a single prompt template — Co-work generates the folder, files, and initial content automatically.

05 · Use cases

From routing to cross-app automation

Once set up, the system enables powerful cross-app workflows:

  • Save a framework — Paste a screenshot and say "figure it out." The routing map sends it to the right file automatically.
  • Draft a follow-up email — Say "I just finished a meeting." Co-work pulls the calendar event, reads the transcript, loads the right workstation, and drafts a follow-up that respects your email conventions.
  • Create a Notion project — "Set up a project in Notion for my Boston trip." Co-work fills every Notion property using your workspace's Notion conventions.

06 · Pro tips

Don't burn through rate limits

Three rules to keep token costs low while getting great results:

Keep root claude.md under 300 lines — It loads every session. Point to resource files only when needed, keep the root lean.

Don't repeat rules across files — Root voice principles set the tone; workstations layer only domain-specific exceptions (greeting, threading).

Default to Sonnet — 80% of tasks don't need Opus. Sonnet costs a fifth and is more than enough. Reserve Opus for tasks with three or more interdependent steps.

The short version

  1. When you smell a winning combination, stop and check the opponent's reply first — Zach's queen sac missed that Black's recapture came with check.
  2. Against a player shoving pawns everywhere, you build solidly and time a pawn break to cash in your development lead. Skipping the break ("no pawn break, no plan") leaves you with nothing to do.
  3. Develop the knight behind the c-pawn, not in front of it — Knight c6 blocks the pawn that should be pressuring the center.
  4. Learn to think in squares: c2-c4 permanently weakens d4, and a knight that can't be kicked off d4 is worth more than the bishop staring at it.

01 · The warm-up trap

Check your opponent's reply before you celebrate

Bartholomew opens with what he calls a trick question, because the mistake in it isn't really a beginner's — it happens at every level up to world champion. White, smelling mate, sacrifices the queen on h6 expecting the rook to mop up. He'd predicted the whole line correctly except for one detail: Black's recapturing bishop hits with check, so Black answers a check with a check and the attack collapses.

The cleaner moves were quiet: Kf2 to sidestep the g-file before reloading the threat, or Rd7 piling a third attacker on the bishop.

The habit to fixOverlooking the opponent's threat while overrating your own. When you sense blood and the sharks are circling, that's exactly the moment to double- and triple-check for the in-between move.

02 · Pawn breaks

Beat a pawn-pusher by opening the position

Game one: Zach plays a Scandinavian that transposes into a French, and his opponent does the thing you see constantly at this level — pushes nearly every pawn. The early errors are coordination ones: e6 locks in the light-square bishop that wanted f5 first, and Knight c6 blocks the c-pawn that should have gone to c5 to pressure d4.

Zach gets it right with the f6 break, then loses the thread.

Instead of opening it, Zach abruptly played f5, slamming the position shut and erasing his own development lead.

No pawn break, no plan.— John Bartholomew, quoting IM Axel Smith
The takeawayAgainst someone who pushes pawns to bother you, don't just develop and wait. Build solidly, time your break, and be ready to cash in. Close the position yourself and the development edge means nothing.

03 · Thinking in squares

c4 weakens d4 forever

Game two is a Sicilian where White avoids the open lines and plays c2-c4. Bartholomew uses it to push the bigger idea: stop thinking only about what your pieces attack and start thinking about squares. Both White pawns now bypass d4, so d4 can never again be challenged by a pawn — it's a permanent outpost for a Black knight.

He frames the strategic dream: trade off White's dark-square bishop, keep your own knight, and the d4 outpost becomes a lasting edge.

The habit to buildIn a closed position with no immediate tactics, evaluate the coming trades. Which piece do you want to keep, which to swap? Here, getting rid of the bishop that guards d4 is worth aiming at for many moves.

05 · Live game two

A better game, undone by a snap move

Game two is real progress — Zach develops cleanly, controls the center, and even wins a couple of pawns. But his king is exposed, and the bishop on b7 plus the enemy queen are quietly aiming at g2.

When the opponent dropped a piece with a strange-looking move, Zach grabbed it instantly instead of asking what it was for. It was a mating setup.

The recurring lessonWhen the opponent plays a weird move, slow down and ask what it threatens. The snap recapture here ignored a double attack on g2.

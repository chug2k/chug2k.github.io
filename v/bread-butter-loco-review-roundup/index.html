The Bread & Butter Loco, According to 16 Reviewers

Sixteen reviewers tested Bread & Butter's first full-foam paddle. Almost all of them landed in the same place — an elite power paddle that gets you ~85-90% of the $300+ Selkirk Boomstick for $180 — and then split, sharply, on whether the feel, the elongated shape, and the six-month warranty hold up.

TL;DR

The 30-second read

  1. Near-unanimous verdict — an elite full-foam power paddle, $199 ($180 with a code), three traditional shapes, that competes with the Selkirk Boomstick at $100-150 less. Multiple reviewers called it a 2025 paddle-of-the-year contender.
  2. Johnkew's lab numbers anchor it: firepower meta 92/94/84 (elongated/hybrid/widebody), spin ~1850-2150 RPM, QC core 0.41-0.42 (high-power category).
  3. The hybrid is the consensus best all-rounder; the widebody is the most forgiving; the elongated is the most disputed (heavy ~120-123 swing weight, and Pickleball Effect would take the Boomstick over it).
  4. Biggest real fight is feel — is it plush/dwelly (Pickleball Medicine, Rafa, Luca) or stiff/poppy and hard to tame (Johnkew, West Side)? Reviewers genuinely disagree, and it depends on shape and break-in.
  5. The honest caveats: only a 6-month warranty vs Selkirk's lifetime, not for sub-4.0 players who can't control elite power, and 11 of these 16 reviews ran a discount code (sponsored/affiliate).

01 · What it is

A foam Boomstick for $180

The Loco is Bread & Butter's first full-foam paddle — a complete Gen 4 redesign of the old Gen 2 Kevlar Loco (which was a control paddle; this one is a power paddle). Reviewers were near-unanimous on the baseline.

Price

$199 retail, ~$179-$180 with a reviewer discount code; West Side measured it ~$133-$153 cheaper than the Selkirk Boomstick.

Shapes

Three traditional shapes — widebody/standard, hybrid, elongated — all 16mm. One of the only full-foam lineups offered in all three.

Core

Floating Gen 4 core — dense EP (expanded polypropylene) center fully encapsulated by a lighter EVA foam ring.

Face

Thermoformed peel-ply raw carbon fiber / fiberglass / carbon fiber (CFC) layup; the fiberglass layer adds pop, stiffness and feedback.

Approval

USAPA-approved only (not UPA/PPA-Challenger dual-approved). No Bible-verse graphic, unlike the Honolulu paddles.

Warranty

Six months on workmanship, plus a 30-day love-it-or-return-it window.

02 · Where reviewers agree

The consensus

Strip out personal taste and almost every reviewer converges on the same handful of findings.

  1. It's an elite power paddleJohnkew puts power/pop percentiles in the high 80s-90s; Matt's measured 90th-95th percentile serves (59.4-60.2 mph); Pursuit, Medicine, STS and New Main all call it top-tier.
  2. ~85-90% of the Boomstick for far less moneyRafa pegged it at "85 to 90%" of Boomstick performance; West Side rated power 9/10 vs Boomstick 9.5-10; the value gap is the single most-repeated point.
  3. Spin is a genuine strengthMeasured 1850-2150 RPM (Johnkew), 2270-2285 (Matt's), 2074-2185 (STS), 2253 (All Drive No Drop) — upper-70s to 80s percentiles, helped by a coarse, grippy peel-ply.
  4. Big, forgiving sweet spot for a power paddleMultiple reviewers, strongest on the widebody/standard; STS rated the standard's sweet spot 9.5.
  5. The hybrid is the all-rounderJohnkew, Pursuit, Matt's, and Pickleball Effect all called the hybrid their favorite or the most balanced of the three.
  6. Responds well to added weight2.5-5g per side at 3/9 (or 4/8) smooths it out and grows the sweet spot — a near-universal recommendation; try it stock first.
  7. It is NOT a control/touch paddleEveryone agrees it's power-first; soft-game players from plush paddles face an adjustment period.

03 · Where they disagree

The real fights

These are the disputes that survive scrutiny — not phrasing differences, but reviewers reaching opposite conclusions on the same paddle.

Feel: plush vs. stiffThe single biggest split. Pickleball Medicine calls it "plush and absorbent" with a "grab-and-throw feel"; Rafa (Tech Dude) says it lets the ball "sit and dwell" for a plush sensation; Luca calls it the spongiest, most Genesis-like EVA-ring paddle. But Johnkew found the ball leaves the face faster (less dwell) due to the stiff fiberglass and "struggled initially to control the pop"; West Side calls it "much stiffer than many other foam paddles" and personally prefers the softer Inferno. Likely explanation: it varies by shape and breaks in softer over time (Matt's noted his more-played hybrid felt softer than less-played shapes).
The elongated shapeMost reviewers find it solid but heavy — swing weights of 118-123 across testers (Pickleball Studio 123, STS 121.2, Matt's 118.6, Luca 118-120) push it into heavy-paddle territory with little room to add weight. Pickleball Effect went further and called the elongated "a disappointment," saying its ~120 swing weight doesn't buy an oversized sweet spot, and he'd take the Boomstick over the elongated Loco outright. Pursuit also rated the elongated the least stable of the three.
Price/value vs. the warranty asteriskThe value case is the loudest pro — Rafa: two Locos cost ~$20-30 more than one Boomstick ($333); West Side: "best price-to-performance Gen 4 paddle right now." But the warranty is the loudest con: just 6 months on workmanship (Pickleball Studio, Matt's, All Drive No Drop all flagged it), versus Selkirk's lifetime coverage and the Honolulu J2 FC Plus's one year. Pickleball Studio noted it's "roughly double the price" of budget foam paddles like the Vatic V7 or Rhombus Quanta.
Does it beat the Boomstick, or just undercut it?Luca (Rackets & Runners) and West Side say the Loco actually beats the Boomstick on feel, soft-spin variety, and control — not just on price. But James Ignatowich, after the hype, "still prefers the Boomstick overall" (bigger sweet spot, more pop, better bottom-of-paddle performance), ranking the Loco a clear second. Johnkew also personally found the Loco's pop harder to tame than the Boomstick's.
Grit durabilityDisputed. Rafa says Loco grit "has been consistently higher than all competitors, so spin may last longer," and New Main saw it hold up over 10-15 hours. But West Side saw grit "noticeably reduced after 3-4 hours," and Ignatowich says it's "normal peel-ply that wears down like any other — no special long-lasting grit." STS, after ~2 months, reported grit and edge guard holding up fine.

04 · The lab anchor

Johnkew Pickleball

Johnkew Pickleball is the most-trusted anchor here for one reason: measured, repeatable lab data (firepower/spin "meta" scores, QC core readings) rather than feel alone. Note this review is sponsored, but its numbers are the load-bearing reference the other reviewers' impressions get checked against.

Firepower meta

92 elongated · 94 hybrid · 84 widebody (high 80s-90s percentiles)

QC core

0.41-0.42 — squarely in the high-power category

Spin

~1850-2150 RPM, spin meta in the 70s

Stock swing weights

121.4 elongated · 115 hybrid · 109 widebody

Construction

floating EP center + EVA band, thermoformed, honeycomb-PP-filled handle, CFC face

Favorite shape

hybrid (more juice than widebody, more forgiving than elongated)

Johnkew's honest knock — and it's a minority opinion he flags as such — is that the stiff fiberglass made the pop harder to control on midcourt resets than the Boomstick. The widebody, with its oversized sweet spot and low swing weight, solved that for him.

The Loco is an elite, high-power full-foam paddle with a wide sweet spot and crisp stiff feel that competes with the Boomstick at over $100 less — best for players comfortable with elite power, but not ideal for those who struggle to control high-power paddles.— Johnkew Pickleball

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy ifYou want elite, near-Boomstick power and spin for $180, you're 4.0+ with technique to generate and control pace, and you want a traditional shape — get the hybrid for balance, the standard/widebody for forgiveness.
  2. Skip ifYou're below ~4.0 or can't tame high-power paddles, you rely on maximum dwell for guided touch shots, you want a long warranty (this is 6 months), or you already own a Boomstick, J2NF, Quanta, or Tornazo — the upgrade isn't compelling.
  3. Honest readThe strongest, most consistent claim across all 16 reviews is value, not dominance — it's ~85-90% of a $300+ paddle for $180. The feel is genuinely divisive (plush to some, stiff to others) and the elongated shape is the weak link. Lab-anchored (Johnkew) and non-sponsored (Ignatowich, West Side, Rafa) voices land the same place: an excellent power paddle and the value pick of its class, a notch below the Boomstick on outright performance.

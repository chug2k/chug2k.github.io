The CRBN TruFoam Genesis, According to 22 Reviewers

Twenty-two of the most-watched YouTube reviews of CRBN's $280 full-foam paddle, reconciled into one read — where the reviewers agree, where they don't, and whose verdict you should weight most.

TL;DR

The 30-second version

  1. First true full-foam core. No polypropylene honeycomb, so no core crush — the single claim every reviewer agrees on. Johnkew's lab logged 10,000+ shots at 50+ mph with zero dead spots and an unchanged Q-core score.
  2. Elite spin, by the numbers. Measured 2,100–2,400 RPM across reviewers (STS hit 2,345; Kev ~2,400) — multiple databases call it the highest-spinning paddle they've tested.
  3. Not a power paddle. All-court at best in stock form; near-universal advice is to add tungsten/lead tape to unlock it. Power hitters coming off a Mod or Gearbox will be underwhelmed.
  4. $279.99 is the fight. Independents repeatedly name cheaper rivals — Honolulu J2NF (~$175–195), Vatic, 11624, Neonic — that match or beat it on value.

01 · What it is

The near-unanimous baseline

Nearly every reviewer agrees on the spec sheet. CRBN spent ~18 months and 200+ prototypes building the first legal 100% foam-core paddle, using a closed-cell foam borrowed from the surfboard industry rather than the polypropylene honeycomb that every other paddle uses.

Price

$279.99 retail; most reviewers carry a 10% code dropping it to ~$252–265.

Core

Single-piece high-density foam, voided (rectangular cutouts at the perimeter, circular in the center). No honeycomb, not thermoformed (the original line is glued/epoxied at room temp).

Face

Raw T700 carbon fiber, peel-ply texture, with a fiberglass layer/patch under the center sweet spot for added pop.

Thickness

14 mm only — but plays closer to a 16 mm feel. CRBN varies foam density, not thickness.

Original shapes

Genesis 1 (elongated, 5.5" handle), Genesis 2 (widebody, 5.25", lowest swing weight ~112–114), Genesis 3 (elongated/hybrid, 5.25"). A later Genesis 4 hybrid (~107 swing weight) arrived months on.

Legal

USAP / UPA / PBCORE 43 certified — no delisting risk, passes the future 0.43 core standard.

02 · Where reviewers agree

The consensus

Across 22 reviews, a handful of conclusions repeat almost word-for-word.

  1. Durability is the headlineNo polypropylene means no core crush. Pickleball Studio saw no change after 6 months / 4 as a main paddle; PickleballCentral saw none down to 20°F; Johnkew's destructive test ran 18 months with no breakage.
  2. Spin is genuinely elite2,100–2,400 RPM measured. STS logged 2,267 / 2,342 / 2,345 RPM on the three shapes — the highest in their database. Driven by peel-ply texture plus long foam dwell time.
  3. It is NOT a power paddle stock"All-court" or "aggressive all-court" is the ceiling. Multiple reviewers say add weight before judging it.
  4. Distinctive dwell/feelThe ball "sits on the face" and pockets. Praised as natural/springy by some, "mushy" by others — but everyone agrees it's unlike poly or EVA foam.
  5. Genesis 2 (widebody) is the crowd pickBest balance of swing weight, twist weight, and sweet spot. STS calls it the "Goldilocks"; Johnkew and Kev both gravitate to it.
  6. No real break-inJohnkew measured only ~1% power / ~3% pop change over weeks. Performance is consistent from day one.

03 · Where they disagree

The genuine disputes

Dispute: Is $279.99 justified?The loudest fight. Pickleball Studio would "really have to think" about spending his own money and names Vatic Pro Saga, Neonic Flare, 11624 Pegasus, and the Honolulu J2NF (~$175 after code, ~$70–100 cheaper) as matching or beating it. Pickleball Effect (Brandon) notes the foam market now spans $100–$333. JJ and Matt's Pickleball decline to recommend it "in a vacuum." On the other side, Paddle Addicts (a CRBN Ambassador) calls $280 "a great price" for the R&D, and Pickle Madness frames it as the only paddle that truly delivers the TruFoam experience.
Dispute: Does it need weight tape to be good?Pickleball Studio is emphatic — stock it feels "lifeless and dull," and he'd pick another paddle if forced to play it unmodified; +7–8" tungsten at 3 & 9 transforms it to compete with the Engage Pursuit One and Paddletek TK. Pickleball Willy says the same for the Genesis 3 ("dinking machine" stock, "laser-like drives" with lead). But PickleballCentral and Building Pickleball found it satisfying largely as-is, and Genesis 4 ships at a low ~107 swing weight specifically so you don't have to.
Dispute: Great feel, or 'lost in the softness'?Pickleball Effect calls it the way "you think a paddle should" feel. Rackets & Runners (Luca) calls the exact same paddle the softest he's ever tested — "mushy, muted," he loses connection to the ball and compares it to a Wilson Clash. Kev and Paddle Addicts warn players who like to "feel where they're hitting" may dislike the muted, near-vibrationless response.
Dispute: The center fiberglass patch — feature or flaw?The patch adds pop on clean center hits, but it only covers the sweet-spot center. Pickleball Studio gets an unpredictable flyer on roughly 1-in-10 net blocks; Matt's Pickleball flags an occasional dead-center "hot spot." Pickleball Effect notes unexpected pop on fast resets. Reviewers want it enlarged to the full face.
Dispute: Durability claim vs. rattles and snapsThe core durability is near-undisputed, but build QC isn't. Pickleball Studio reports user accounts of loose glue rattling in the voids (and a few handle snaps); Pickleball Studio's Carbon 4 unit developed a rattle after 2–3 hours. He calls a rattle "unacceptable at $280" even when it doesn't hurt performance. Most reviewers never experienced it personally.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball's lab read

Johnkew is the most-trusted voice here because he tests rather than just plays. His launch review (sponsored) places the Genesis honestly: more power than the older CRBN X Series but low in the power category — below Paddletek Bantam and the Yola 3S, comparable firepower to the Spartus Olympus and Franklin C45. Measured TFG1 swing weight 124 (90th pct power, 76th pct pop, but 21st pct twist weight = narrow sweet spot). Break-in moved power ~1% and pop ~3%. Spin loss after weeks was only 100–200 RPM vs. the 300+ typical for raw carbon. Gripes: heavier than the X Series, a squishy foam handle he dislikes, narrow sweet spots on the elongated shapes. He prefers the widebody TFG2 and says add 3g tungsten at 9 & 3.

His second appearance is the most load-bearing data point on this page: in a later Barrage review, the Genesis is his durability control paddle.

The Genesis has received over 10,000 shots at 50+ mph with no dead spots and no performance drop — its Q-core score is identical to when it was brand new. CRBN's true foam cores are where they truly stand out as a brand.— Johnkew Pickleball

05 · Independent voices

The non-sponsored reviewers

Thirteen of the 22 reviews are independent (not sponsored, no giveaway flag). They cluster around the same verdict with sharper edges:

  1. Pickleball Studio (3 reviews, no sponsorship)The most rigorous independent. Came around to paying full $280 after 6 months — but only weighted, and only after naming half-price rivals. NOT a power paddle; subpar stock handle ("pretty garbage").
  2. STS Pickleball (8aKtyKf4dPA, independent)Numeric scores — Genesis 2: 9.0 power / 9.4 sweet spot / 9.3 control; record 2,345 RPM. "Carbon did a wonderful job." Negatives: unfamiliar "soft wood" feel and the high price.
  3. Pickleball Effect / Bren"Most promising new core design," possibly the future. TF2 is the pick; higher launch angle is a real learning curve.
  4. Rackets & Runners / LucaThe dissent — objectively elite control and a massive sweet spot, but personally a deal-breaker: "mushy," lost connection, preferred even the Yola Pro 4 and Gearbox.
  5. Pickleball WarehouseLab-measured the Genesis 4 (Head 4-in-1, 10 cm above grip); balanced power/control, rounded top expands the sweet spot. Pocketing feel near the Gearbox Pro Ultimate but safer.
  6. JJ Pickleball (flagged sponsored via code, but speed-tested)Radar data — Genesis 1 (weighted) 52.6 mph, below the Yola 3S (53.6), Proton Flamingo (55.3), Paddletek TKO CX (53.7). Loses on power; wins on spin/control.
  7. Pickle MadnessGenesis vs. a $40 Alibaba clone — the real one wins on feel, stability, spin predictability, and is the only USAP-legal option.
  8. Pickleball WillyGenesis 3 "beast" after 10 hours, but underweighted out of the box; lead tape required for power hitters.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy ifYou shape the ball (heavy topspin, tennis/semi-western grip), want elite spin and a long-dwell control feel, are tired of core crush, and you're willing to add tungsten tape — or just buy the Genesis 4 for a lower stock swing weight. Genesis 2 (widebody) is the safest pick.
  2. Skip ifYou want plug-and-play power (Mod / Gearbox / Yola 3S buyers will be let down), you need very fast hands and hate high swing weight, you rely on crisp tactile feedback (the muted feel divides reviewers), or you're price-sensitive — cheaper rivals match it.
  3. Honest readA genuine engineering first whose durability is real and whose spin is best-in-class, sold at a top-of-market $279.99 that the independents struggle to justify on value alone. It's a spin-and-feel paddle wearing a power-paddle price tag. Try it weighted before you decide, and use the 30-day trial.

01 · The paddle

What the Enhance MPP Turbo actually is

Across all 22 reviews the baseline specs are near-unanimous. It is a Gen-4 full-foam paddle built around an MPP (microcellular polypropylene) core — chemically foamed nano-cells rather than the expanded beads of EPP — floating inside an EVA perimeter ring, with a CFC (carbon / fiberglass / carbon) face on raw carbon peel-ply.

Price

$119-120 retail (one source lists $110); $99-100 with virtually every reviewer's discount code.

Core

MPP foam — billed as the first sub-$100 MPP paddle on the market; floating core inside an EVA band.

Face

CFC layup (carbon-fiberglass-carbon) on raw carbon fiber; no long-lasting grit coating.

Shape

Elongated only for MPP (~16.5 x 7.375-7.5 in). Wide-body and hybrid exist only on the cheaper EPP Turbo.

Swing weight

clustered ~115-118 across testers; twist weight ~6.1-6.4 (elongated).

Tuning

internal weights ~3 g at 3:00 and 9:00 for out-of-box stability.

Coverage

USAP/USAPA certified, 1-year warranty, 14-day trial. Not dual USAPA/UPAA approved.

02 · Consensus

Where reviewers agree

Strip out personal taste and a tight consensus remains. These points show up again and again, from sponsored and independent reviewers alike.

  1. It is a budget InfernoThe dominant theme. Pickleball Pursuit, Johnkew, Pickleball Studio, The Sweet Spot, Matthew K, The Volley Zone and others all say it plays "super similar" to the Lux/Legacy Inferno — estimates range 80% (UnforcedErrors), 90% (Pickleball Tech Dude), to 90-95% (Johnkew's John+Clayton).
  2. High power, high popUniversally a power paddle. Pop often rated slightly ahead of power. PickleBaller ranked it #1 for power among four budget rivals (ahead of Ronbus Quanta, Vatic V-Sol Pro, Friday Aura).
  3. The $99 price is the headlineCalled "insane value," "a steal," "chef's kiss," "no-brainer," "plays like a $250 paddle." Pickleball Studio: best $100 power paddle on the market.
  4. Stiff/hollow MPP feelA loud, thocky, hollow, slightly springy response with some dwell/pocketing — distinct from dense EPP paddles. Liked by most, divisive for some.
  5. Good stock stability and sweet spotThe 3-and-9 internal weighting means most reviewers needed no lead tape; sweet spot repeatedly called large and forgiving.
  6. The fiberglass is barely thereDespite the CFC layup, reviewers expected a fiberglass "spring" and mostly couldn't find it — Johnkew "almost didn't notice it," The Sweet Spot felt only "a tad different."

03 · Disputes

Where reviewers genuinely disagree

This is where the page earns its keep. The fights are real and they map cleanly onto playstyle and tester rigor.

Control: forgiving or punishing?The sharpest split. Pickleball Tech Dude was "shocked" at how much control he got and picks it over the Inferno. The Kitchen and All Drive No Drop say drops/dinks/resets held up fine. But Pickleball Studio (sponsored) struggled — "kept hitting the back wall on counters," more unforced errors — and would not main it. Matt's Pickleball and PickleballPlaybook agree: it does NOT bail out your mishits and is not for a "resetty" player. PickleBaller ranked it dead last (#4) for control among four paddles. Verdict: the paddle rewards good mechanics and punishes weak ones.
Spin: elite or merely adequate?UnforcedErrors calls the spin "elite." Matt's measured above-average RPM. All Drive No Drop measured mid-2200s RPM (solid, just below elite 2300-2400 paddles). But Pickleball Studio and PickleballPlaybook call spin a real weakness — "lacking," "average for a top-tier power paddle" — and warn it loses to newer long-lasting-grit faces like the Vapor Power 2 and Spartus P1. The consistent thread: no long-lasting grit, so spin retention is the question, not raw spin.
Durability: the open riskNobody reported a failure in hand, but the most rigorous voices flag the same concern. Pickleball Studio: MPP cores "historically disbond from the face at a higher rate and faster" than EPP/Gen-3. The Pickleball Dad: MPP foam "is known to disbond." Pickleball Tech Dude saw face-sheet disbonding on the EPP wide-body variant (not the MPP). Counterpoint: Johnkew argues MPP may be MORE durable (no beads to crush), and several note the 1-year warranty covers disbonding. Unproven either way — buy with the warranty in mind.
Price/value: contested only on spin-futureAlmost nobody disputes the value. The lone caveat comes from Pickleball Studio, who agrees it's the best $100 power paddle but warns that as cheap long-grit faces proliferate, this paddle's spin ceiling "will feel more and more limiting on a paddle that hits this hard."

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball, measured

Johnkew Pickleball is the most-trusted source here because his reviews are lab-instrumented (Q-core energy-transfer maps, swing/twist-weight bins, face-consistency curves), not just on-court feel. Two of his videos are in this set: a measured solo review (sponsored — he discloses a commission on code JohnQ, no payment, no editorial input) and an unsponsored sit-down with guest Clayton.

His numbers: power and pop both land "high," with pop slightly ahead; Q-core clusters 0.416-0.419 (middle of the high-power band); swing weight, balance, and twist weight all upper-end of medium — above-average stability, average maneuverability. The Q-core face map is "very flat," dropping off only slightly around the 5-6 inch region. Spin sits at the low end of the high range. Feel quadrant B: stiff and hollow, more hollow than stiff.

His head-to-head: it plays so close to the Lux Inferno (Lozano) that "most players would have a very hard time telling them apart" — the Inferno holds a slight edge in face consistency and the MPP Turbo's faint fiberglass spring is occasionally less intuitive for his style. The John+Clayton video pegs it at 90-95% of the Inferno, with the only open question being whether its break-in profile matches the Inferno's beloved one.

At $100 with code it is an outstanding budget performance paddle — you would be hard-pressed to find a better performance-to-value proposition at this price. It's my personal favorite of the four-paddle Turbo lineup.— Johnkew Pickleball

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy it ifYou drive and attack, want top-tier power and pop on a budget, like a stiff/hollow Inferno-style feel, and want a paddle that's tournament-ready out of the box. At $99 with code it is — by near-consensus — the best value power paddle in the $100 tier.
  2. Skip it ifYou need a paddle to bail out your reset/soft game, you want elite long-lasting grit (look at Vapor Power 2 / Spartus P1), you need dual USAPA/UPAA approval, or you want a wide-body/hybrid (MPP is elongated only).
  3. The honest readThis is a genuinely good paddle that happens to cost $99, not a $99 paddle graded on a curve. It is ~80-95% of a $200-230 Inferno. The two real unknowns are spin longevity (no long-lasting grit) and MPP disbonding durability — both partly covered by the 1-year warranty. If you have solid mechanics, it's an easy yes; if the paddle needs to do the work for you, look elsewhere.

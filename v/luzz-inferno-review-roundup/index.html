The Luzz Inferno, According to 22 Reviewers

Twenty-two YouTube reviewers hit the Luzz Inferno — a Gen-4 foam power paddle that keeps getting compared to the $295 Selkirk Boomstick at roughly $100 less. We read every claim, weighted the lab-tested and unpaid voices highest, and sorted the consensus from the genuine fights.

TL;DR

The 30-second read

  1. It's a Boomstick-feel foam paddle for ~$100 less. A Gen-4 elongated power paddle, 16.5 x 7.5 in, 16 mm core, ~7.9 oz, swing weight ~119 — that nearly everyone compares to the Selkirk Boomstick at a much lower price (~$195 after code vs ~$295).
  2. The MPP foam core is the whole story. A micro-porous-polymer floating core (with an EVA ring) instead of the usual EP foam — credited for high dwell time, a huge sweet spot, and a loud, hollow boom. Johnkew called it a hybrid of the Boomstick and Bread & Butter Loco.
  3. Power and spin are not in dispute; feel is. Matt's Pickleball measured 59.5 mph serve (≈80th pct) and 2274 RPM spin (76th pct). The fight is over the muted/hollow feel — some call it plush and forgiving, others call it dead, like hitting a board.
  4. Two real warnings: break-in and durability. The paddle changes feel for 10-20 hours, which makes evaluation messy. Edge-guard paint scratches easily, early units 'crunched,' and Luzz is China-based despite a US claim — relevant for warranty.
  5. Built from 22 reviews; 14 are sponsored or giveaway-affiliated. The unpaid, lab-tested voices (Johnkew, Matt's) still land net-positive — but the loudest 'best paddle ever' praise mostly comes from reviewers using a discount code.

01 · The paddle

What the Luzz Inferno actually is

Across all 22 reviewers the baseline is near-unanimous: a Gen-4, elongated, foam-core power paddle that Luzz positions as a budget answer to the Selkirk Boomstick. The numbers below are the consensus spec sheet (Matt's Pickleball, West Side, Womack, BK, The Honest Take, Famous by Friday all converge here).

Shape & size

Elongated only, 16.5 in long x 7.5 in wide, 5.5-in handle, ~4.125-in grip. No hybrid or widebody — a repeated complaint.

Core

16 mm "floating" full-foam core using MPP / MPPP (micro-porous polymer) foam with an EVA perimeter ring — not the usual EP foam.

Face

Raw T700 carbon fiber; several call it among the grittiest faces they've used. No fiberglass layer.

Weight

Static ~7.8-8.0 oz; swing weight ~119 (±3); twist weight ~6.1-6.3.

Price

Lists at $229-230; lands ~$166-200 after a reviewer discount code (~$194-195 is the common figure).

Certification

Dual-certified (USAP/UPA) and tournament eligible; one-year warranty (a ~$20 two-year extension is sold separately).

02 · Consensus

Where the reviewers agree

Strip out the personal taste and a remarkably consistent picture remains. These points show up across sponsored and unpaid reviewers alike.

  1. It's a genuine power paddleEffectively every reviewer files it as power-first. BK, Tickle My Pickleball, and Pickle Pick call it the most powerful in the Luzz lineup; The Paddle Plug and The Honest Take rate power 9/10.
  2. The sweet spot is hugeThe single most-repeated praise. Martin Grunin, ItsEZ, Pickleball Willy, Famous by Friday and others describe a large, consistent sweet spot with little pop drop-off toward the edges — unusual for a power paddle.
  3. It plays like a cheaper BoomstickWest Side ("closest any paddle gets to the Boomstick"), Matt's, BK, Womack, and Playbook all anchor it to the Boomstick at ~$100 less. Playbook: "a Boomstick and a Loco had a baby."
  4. MPP foam = more dwell than typical foamBK, Scooter, The New Main, Pickleball Willy agree the micro-porous core gives more dwell time and ball-shaping than the stiff foam paddles it competes with.
  5. Spin is strongFrom Matt's measured 76th-percentile RPM to BK's "top fifth percentile" to Famous by Friday's "late bite," spin lands consistently above average.
  6. It needs a break-inPickleball Effect, Martin Grunin, Pickle Pick, Pickleball Advocate, and The New Main all report it starts stiff/poppy and becomes softer and dwellier after roughly 1-20 hours.

03 · Disputes

Where they genuinely disagree

This is where the roundup earns its keep. Three real fights, not just taste.

Fight 1 — Is the feel plush or dead?The biggest split. The positive camp (BK: "plush"; Martin Grunin: best-in-class resets; The New Main: arm-friendly, lots of dwell) loves the muted, hollow feel. The negative camp says the same trait kills feedback: Tickle My Pickleball said it felt "similar to hitting a wooden board" and lacked ball pocketing; Pickleball Effect found it unremarkable out of the box; The New Main's own reviewer personally preferred the crisper Bread & Butter Loco. Same paddle, opposite verdicts — it's an acquired taste.
Fight 2 — Is ~$195-230 worth it?The value case is contested almost entirely along sponsorship lines. Unpaid Matt's Pickleball says it undercuts comparable foam builds by $50-75 and is a fair Boomstick alternative. But unpaid skeptics push back hard: Pickleball Effect ($230 "too expensive," buy the Loco); The Sweet Spot (the $99 Enhance Turbo MPP plays similarly — buy two for the price of one); Scooter (prefers the cheaper Luzz Cannon); Pickleball Advocate (picked the $150 GatorStrike over it on price alone). Sponsored reviewers, who carry 10-15% codes, near-uniformly call it "good value."
Fight 3 — Control under pressureReviewers split on whether the big sweet spot helps or hurts touch. Martin Grunin and Playbook call resets best-in-class. But Pickle Li says it resets worse than other Gen-4s due to extra pop; The New Main got more pop-ups on dinks once broken in; ItsEZ found it "the best paddle I ever touched" yet "very hard to control" once his touch was off or with a fast ball. The trampoline pop is a gift for the confident and a liability for the shaky.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball (unpaid, measured)

Johnkew is the most-trusted reference point here: not sponsored, no giveaway, and the channel known for radar and Q-core lab data (full numbers were still pending at the time of this early-impressions video). His read frames the whole roundup.

Core, observed

Full floating MPP foam center with an EVA band around the perimeter.

The defining comparison

A hybrid feel between the Selkirk Boomstick and the Bread & Butter Loco — boomy, loud (as loud or louder than the Boomstick).

Power placement

Firepower in the Bread & Butter Loco range — just below the Boomstick, above paddles like the Honolulu. Easily accessible power with good control for its level.

Feel

On the hollower side for a full-foam paddle, but not as hollow as Cannon / Gen-3 honeycomb paddles. Good sweet spot.

Price noted

~$180 after code — notably higher than the Luzz Cannon ($94).

It was my favorite of the three Luzz paddles — it connected more than the Tornazzo because of that familiar feel, power and pop profile. A great paddle.— Johnkew Pickleball

07 · Verdict

Who should actually buy it

  1. Buy it ifYou want Boomstick-style power, a huge forgiving sweet spot, and a loud hollow boom — for ~$100 less than a Boomstick. Best for 3.5+ intermediate-to-advanced players, tennis converts, and anyone who drives, counters, and resets with confident touch. Plan to weight it (many add 3-9 g) and break it in 10-20 hours.
  2. Skip it ifYou play a touch/dink-first soft game and need crisp contact feedback (the muted feel hides information); you're below 3.5 (the pop is hard to tame); you want a hybrid/widebody shape (elongated only); or you're price-sensitive — the Bread & Butter Loco, Vatic Vole Pro, Enhance Turbo MPP ($99), and even the Luzz Cannon are cheaper near-substitutes.
  3. The honest readThis is a very good foam power paddle, not a category-defining one. The measured, unpaid voices (Johnkew, Matt's) land it as a legitimate sub-$200 Boomstick alternative with strong power and spin. The "best paddle ever" ceiling comes mostly from reviewers holding a discount code. Two non-taste risks are real: a long, messy break-in and durability/support question marks (scratch-prone edge, early crunching, China-based support despite a US claim).

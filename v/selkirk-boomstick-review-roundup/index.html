The Selkirk Boomstick, According to 21 Reviewers

Twenty-one pickleball reviewers — from lab-testing channels to pros to retailer promos — put Selkirk's $333 Boomstick through its paces. They agree on the physics and disagree, loudly, on whether anyone should pay for it.

TL;DR

The 30-second read

  1. The Boomstick is a genuinely elite power paddle — near-top-of-market power, ~2300 RPM spin, and the largest sweet spot most reviewers have ever felt (widebody twist weight hits the 100th percentile of Johnkew's database).
  2. It is the most expensive paddle on the market at $333, and that is the single biggest point of contention — discount codes only add a $40 gift card, they do not lower the price.
  3. The full-foam core (EP foam + EVA ring) means no break-in and no core-crush; the open question is the Infinigrit surface and the externally-mounted MOI weights, which several reviewers tie to edge-guard durability concerns.
  4. Independent grit-durability data is split: STS measured only a 15–18 RPM drop after a month (vs. a typical 70–100), while West Side and Ignatowich report the grit smoothing out and spin dropping within days to weeks.
  5. Near-unanimous fit: buy it if you are an aggressive 4.0+ power player and price is no object; skip it if you live in the soft game, play below ~4.0, or care about value — cheaper rivals (Honolulu foams, Gearbox GX2, Yola Pro 4) get you most of the way for $50–$130 less.

01 · What it is

A $333 full-foam power paddle

Across all 21 reviews the baseline is remarkably consistent. The Boomstick is a Selkirk Labs power paddle that pairs a foam core with a spray-on grit surface and a first-of-its-kind external weighting system.

Price

$333 — the most expensive paddle on the market, called out by nearly every reviewer. Discount codes (INF-*) add a $40 Selkirk gift card; a $33 rebate is available for leaving feedback within 60 days. Neither lowers the sticker.

Core

Gen 4 full-foam "Boom Core" — an expanded-polypropylene (EP) foam center surrounded by a softer EVA foam power ring. No honeycomb in the hitting zone, so no core crush.

Shapes

Two — elongated (~16.5 in) and widebody (~16 in), both 16 mm thick with an extra-long 5.8 in handle (some reviewers measured ~5.5–5.75 in).

Surface

Infinigrit, Selkirk's spray-on grit over carbon fiber, which Selkirk claims lasts ~3x longer than raw carbon fiber (ASTM testing per Johnkew).

MOI tuning

First-of-its-kind external perimeter weighting — two 7.5 g weights (~15–15.5 g total) clipped at the 3 and 9 o'clock positions. Effectively permanent; removing them risks tearing the edge guard.

Warranty

Limited lifetime — upgraded from the usual 2-year Labs coverage because the Boomstick is a permanent Labs-only product.

02 · Where reviewers agree

The consensus is the physics

Strip out price and the reviewers converge hard. These are the points repeated across both independent and sponsored channels.

  1. Elite power and popMultiple lab-style measurements put it at or near the legal power limit (Johnkew elongated QC core 0.431, top of high-power; Matt's Firepower Index 89–91). Reviewers consistently rank pop even higher than raw power.
  2. Top-tier spin (~2300 RPM)Johnkew ~2300, Matt's 2,294 (elong) / 2,188 (wide), STS 2,319 (elong) / 2,245 (wide), All Drive No Drop ~2,200. Elite, with a unique dual-unidirectional carbon layup that flexes along the long axis like vertical tennis strings.
  3. A record-class sweet spotWidebody twist weight at the 100th percentile of Johnkew's database; West Side calls it the biggest sweet spot of any paddle reviewed, stock or weighted. Low swing weight + very high twist weight = fast hands and forgiveness rare for a power paddle.
  4. No break-in, consistent over timeThe foam core plays the same day 1 and day 100 — no core crush, no delamination, no hotting-up. Building Pickleball cites Selkirk's "12% more exit speed out of the box" claim.
  5. It is loudThe "boom" is real — many reviewers call it the loudest paddle they have heard, on both soft and hard contact.
  6. Soft game needs adjustmentEven fans agree blocks, resets, and dinks require dialing in out of the box because of how poppy it is. Where they split is whether that adjustment is easy or a dealbreaker.

03 · Where they disagree

The real fights

Three genuine disputes run through the corpus. The reviewers are looking at the same paddle and reaching opposite conclusions.

Dispute 1 — Is $333 justifiable?The largest split. The "no" camp is led by non-sponsored voices: Matt's Pickleball ("can't recommend at $333," buy a $200 paddle and spend the rest on lessons), SupaSenpai (overpriced given durability worries), West Side (would not buy it today when cheaper rivals match it), and Building Pickleball (sponsored, but still says drop the price ~$50). The "yes" camp — Rackets & Runners ("ridiculously expensive" but performance backs it), Dinking And Driving ("a no-brainer if it fits your budget"), and Ty Butson (cheaper long-term because it lasts) — argues the lifetime warranty and durability make it "cry once, buy once."
Dispute 2 — Does the Infinigrit grit actually last?Selkirk's whole premium pitch rests on ~3x grit longevity, and reviewers measured opposite results. STS Pickleball logged only a 15–18 RPM drop after a month of heavy daily use (vs. a typical 70–100 RPM monthly drop) — strong support for the claim. But Ignatowich saw grit wear in the sweet spot after just ~4–5 days, and West Side reports Boomsticks on the wall that have "smoothed out noticeably" with a measurable spin drop, now beaten by newer durable-grit surfaces (J2CR Crystal Blue, Harachi hex grit). Matt's splits the difference: the grit does smooth out, but foam-core dwell time keeps spin usable anyway.
Dispute 3 — Edge-guard durability and the MOI weightsThe external MOI weights concentrate force on a small area of the edge guard, and several reviewers tie failures to it. SupaSenpai calls edge-guard peeling the most frequently reported problem and cites online reports of the neck breaking where two materials meet. Pickleball Studio and Matt's both had early units with the edge guard separating near the weight mounts (Selkirk replaced both, blaming early production). Johnkew confirms removing the weights loosened the guard "more easily than expected." Counterpoints: Ignatowich (hundreds of Selkirks, never a structural failure) and Chur2rCPxJc's West Side review say newer units appear to have fixed it (build 8/10).
Dispute 4 — Dwell time and resetsReviewers can't even agree on the basic feel. Matt's, Johnkew, Ty Butson, and Dinking And Driving praise long dwell time that aids control and resets. But West Side and Pickle Li describe "little to no dwell time" — a hollow paddle where the ball leaves immediately and spin is purely grit-driven. Rackets & Runners lands in between: shorter dwell than Gen 3, which makes scramble resets its single biggest flaw.

04 · The anchor

Johnkew Pickleball's lab read

Weighted most heavily here: Johnkew is non-sponsored and the only source giving full lab measurements. His numbers anchor the consensus above.

Power

Elongated QC core 0.431 (top of high-power category); widebody 0.411 (middle). Hits harder than the Yola Pro 4, Paddletech Bantom, and Gearbox GX2 Power.

Spin

~2,300 RPM on both shapes; Infinigrit tests at 3x the durability of raw carbon in ASTM testing.

Sweet spot

Widebody twist weight at the 100th percentile of his database — a maximum sweet spot.

Build

MOI weights securely set in electroplated shells (~15.5 g, likely copper); a gap between EP and EVA layers prevents separation over time. But removing the weights loosened the edge guard more easily than expected.

At $333 it is the hardest sell. It is justified mainly by Infinigrit surface durability and Selkirk's lifetime warranty and service — buyers who don't need those can get comparable performance from cheaper alternatives like the Honolulu full-foams or the Gearbox GX2 Power.— Johnkew Pickleball

06 · The sponsored / giveaway voices

Read with the thumb on the scale (8 of 21)

Eight of the 21 reviews were sponsored or involved a giveaway/affiliate-kickback relationship — including Selkirk's own channel and a retailer. They are not worthless (STS contributes the best grit-longevity measurement), but their verdicts skew positive and should be weighted accordingly.

  1. Selkirk Sport (brand's own channel)Reviewer's "all-time favorite paddle." Naturally the most positive; useful only for the spec/marketing framing (Infinigrit 3x, lifetime warranty).
  2. Pickleball Studio (sponsored)Among the best since Project 002, but explicit: most viewers under 4.5 will play worse with it; one early unit's edge guard came loose.
  3. STS Pickleball (affiliate kickback)The standout data point — measured only a 15–18 RPM grit drop after a month. Would personally pick it over the Pro 4 / GX2 / Flick F1.
  4. Pickleball Medicine (affiliate)Dissection + drop test. Pop rated #1; foam core and MOI system extremely resilient, but the carbon face cracked after ~30 forceful drops.
  5. Building Pickleball (free units)"One of the most complete paddles" — yet still argues the $333 price will hurt Selkirk and they should cut it ~$50.
  6. Pickleball Warehouse (retailer)Likely the most durable paddle on the market; pitch ends with their 30-day return policy. Heavy-feel caveat.
  7. West Side (Omni comparison, sponsored)Most consistent paddle on the market, gigantic sweet spot — but little dwell time and both reviewers would consider other paddles before paying $333.
  8. All Drive No Drop (affiliate code)S-tier power, ~2,200 RPM, would tournament-play it — but says stick with your current power paddle; the premium over GX2 / Pro 4 / Honolulu NF is hard to justify.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy it

  1. Buy it ifYou are an aggressive 4.0+ power player who drives and counters, you want a record-size sweet spot with elite power and ~2300 RPM spin, and price genuinely isn't the deciding factor — the lifetime warranty and no-break-in foam core are the "cry once, buy once" case.
  2. Skip it ifYou live in the soft game (reset- and dink-heavy), play below ~4.0, need a quiet paddle, or care about value — Honolulu full-foams, the Gearbox GX2 Power (~$50 less), the Yola Pro 4, or even ~$99–$170 rivals (EP/MPP Turbo, J6CR, Friday Aura Pro, Draco) get most of the performance for far less.
  3. The honest readThe Boomstick is the paddle that defined the 2025 power arms race and it still plays like an elite. The disagreement isn't really about how it plays — it's about whether Selkirk's durability-and-warranty pitch is worth a $50–$130 premium, and whether the Infinigrit grit and edge guard hold up. The lab-tested, non-sponsored consensus: a brilliant paddle, a hard value.

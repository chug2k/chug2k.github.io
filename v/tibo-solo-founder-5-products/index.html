TL;DR

The bootstrapper's playbook

  1. Five AI products, $1M+/month — Rabbit (AI video shorts, ~$600k/mo with ~4 people) and Outrank (SEO) lead, treated like a diversified portfolio where a small bet can become the biggest.
  2. Validate with revenue, not vanity. The #1 mistake is caring more about not-being-wrong than being right — and when users twist your product into something else, follow that signal (it's how Typeframe became Rabbit). Charge from day one.
  3. SEO is his top channel and it's not dead: build domain authority, then a dedicated landing page per tool/use-case — these are 'AI-resilient' because an AI snippet can't replace an actual tool.
  4. Run lean: one person can do the work of 20 now, so a tiny team can pivot fast. Build with Cursor (swapping whichever model is best that week); AI writes specs, and he'll discard ~half rather than fight them.
  5. Price premium ($50–100/mo) for low churn and good customers; aim for <20% monthly churn before scaling acquisition. And ship in quantity — 9 products failed, the 10th took off; quantity ends up producing the best quality.

01 · The portfolio

Five products, $1M+/month

Tibo runs five AI products, nearly all riding the AI-video and SEO waves. He treats them like a diversified portfolio — and points out that today's small bet can become tomorrow's biggest line.

02 · Validate, don't force-sell

Follow the signal

The core mindset: your job is to validate, not to sell. The trap is caring more about not being wrong than about being successful — so you force the sale and miss the bigger opportunity users are pointing at.

When you have a product that's made for one thing and people are actually twisting it to turn it into this other thing, that's usually a very strong signal you have to follow.— Tibo Louis-Lucas

That signal is exactly how the product-video tool Typeframe became Rabbit.

03 · Rabbit

Stitching 5-second clips into real videos

Rabbit's origin (2024): AI models only made 5-second clips, and stitching them coherently was painful. Rabbit does it automatically — splitting a long video into scenes with consistent characters and environments.

04 · Talk to users, ship experiments

A firehose of feedback

His unlock for finding what to build: a direct line to users (early on, the in-app support button went straight to his Twitter DMs). That firehose reveals the core problem. Alongside it, he ships many tiny one-page tools to test demand.

Where ideas come fromLook at the products you already pay for and rebuild a more specific version for your own weird use case — odds are many others share it. And it's completely fine to aim for $10k/month; that's freedom, not failure.

05 · SEO that survives AI

A landing page per tool

SEO is his #1 channel and very much alive. Two parts: first, build domain authority (hard below DR~20 — which is why Outrank's backlink marketplace is valuable); second, beat AI snippets with tools.

06 · Running five businesses lean

One person, the work of 20

He keeps the team tiny (a few contractors) — convinced one person can now do the work of 20, which makes pivoting fast and cheap (vs. the "insane energy" of re-convincing a 20-person team).

07 · Price premium, obsess over churn

He prices backwards: find an idea he can charge $50–100/month for, then shape the product to fit. That band is the sweet spot — high enough for quality customers and low churn, low enough that nobody asks for a quote.

08 · Quantity beats quality

Ship until something sticks

The counterintuitive finish: he once shipped one new product a week for four months — nine failed, the tenth took off. Treat every release as an experiment; you genuinely can't predict the winners (his own surprised him).

The Friday Aura, According to 16 Reviewers

Friday's first full-foam paddles — the $129 Aura and $169 Aura Pro — landed to a wave of reviews. We read 16 of them, lab data included, to separate the consensus from the disputes.

Creator: clpanicPublished: 2026-06-04 05:13:30+00:00
Watch on YouTube
TL;DR

The 30-second version

  1. The Aura ($129) is the soft, dense, all-court control paddle; the Aura Pro ($169) is the stiff, hollow, high-power one. Same family, two different game modes.
  2. Near-universal praise for value, spin, and a huge sweet spot, plus a big build-quality jump over the old Friday Fever.
  3. Johnkew's lab puts the Pro mid-pack in the high-power category (Q Core 0.419, between the Les Inferno and B&B Loco) with spin in the upper-half of high.
  4. Real disagreements: stock stability/twist weight (several add weight), durability of the non-durable grit, and whether $169 is a steal or merely fine in a crowded field.
  5. 13 of 16 reviews were sponsored or giveaway-seeded. The honest read leans hardest on the three non-sponsored reviews and the lab numbers.

01 · The paddles

What Friday actually shipped

Two Gen 4 full-foam paddles, launched March 6th. The Aura is the all-court value option; the Aura Pro is the power flagship built around a carbon frame. Nearly every reviewer agreed on the baseline below.

Aura — $129

All-court foam paddle (about $119 after affiliate codes). Three shapes from day one: widebody, hybrid, elongated.

Aura Pro — $169

Power foam paddle (about $159 after codes). Launched elongated-only; hybrid and widebody followed by pre-order (mid-April per several reviewers).

Core

Dual-density EPP + EVA foam with a rubber perimeter strip Friday calls Elastatech / "nonna's fettuccine" (patent-pending). Artificial cartilage foam (ACF) in the throat for vibration.

Aura Pro frame

Double-thermoformed, built around a rigid hollow blow-molded carbon-fiber frame (~30g) running the full perimeter and into the handle.

Faces

Aura uses a CFC (carbon-fiberglass-carbon) layup; Aura Pro is carbon-fiber-only. Both 16mm with a coarse peel-ply gritty texture.

Elongated specs

16.5 x ~7.4 in, ~8.1 oz, swing weight ~115–117, twist weight ~5.8–6.1, USAP-certified.

02 · Where reviewers agree

The consensus

Across sponsored and non-sponsored reviews alike, the same handful of points came up again and again.

  1. Two distinct paddlesAura = soft/dense, more dwell, control-leaning. Aura Pro = stiff/hollow, poppy, power-leaning. West Side called them "two game modes."
  2. Spin is a strengthAbove-average to elite across the board. All Drive No Drop measured the Pro at 2314 (elite) on its spin metric; Matt's logged the Pro surface at 8.54 microns (among the roughest in his database).
  3. Big, forgiving sweet spotRepeatedly compared to the Bread & Butter Loco and Boomstick. Foam construction means no honeycomb cells to break down.
  4. Real valueSub-$170 for the Pro and ~$129 for the Aura was called a genuine bargain by almost everyone, sponsored or not.
  5. Massive build-quality jumpMultiple reviewers flagged the Aura line as night-and-day better than the old Friday Fever — no cheap paint, better grip, premium feel.
  6. Most are weightableSeveral reviewers added a gram-per-inch of tape, especially on the Pro, to firm up stability and plow-through.

03 · Where they disagree

The genuine disputes

Strip away the sponsored gloss and three real arguments remain.

Dispute 1 — Is the Pro stable enough stock?Sharpest split in the set. Austin Hardy (Playbook) measured twist weight at 5.77 — below Friday's listed 6.15 and Matt's ~6 — and said the Pro is "a bit twisty and not very stable," with ~20% of his counters going slightly long until he added side weight. Pickleball Effect called average twist weight his "biggest concern" but fixed it with 5.5 in of half-gram tape. By contrast, Matt's Pickleball never added weight to the elongated and "never felt the need." So: fine stock for some, needs weight for others.
Dispute 2 — Durability of the gritThe texture is not durable grit (it is carbon fiber). West Side (sponsored long-term review) said it still felt gritty after 7+ hours but will smooth out. Pickleball Studio named the lack of long-lasting grit "the main thing holding it back given current buyer priorities" — for ~$40–50 more a Spartus P1 or Vapor Power 2 gets you durable grit. All Drive No Drop and West Side both flagged "elite spin now, but it'll fade."
Dispute 3 — Is $169 a steal or just fine?Pickleball Studio (non-sponsored) called the Pro's $169 "fantastic — still great even at $230–250." But they also mapped brutal competition on both sides: go up ~$40–50 for better grit, go down for enhanced MPP, EP Turbos, Vatic Pro Vis, or Quant. PickleBaller noted the Aura is ~$20 more than the MPP Turbo, V7 Pro, and Quanta it lost to on power. Great value, crowded neighborhood.
Side note — the widebody Pro is a different animalPickleball Studio's early-impressions crew found the widebody Aura Pro sits soft-and-hollow while the hybrid and elongated are stiff-and-hollow — unusual for one paddle line. One host loved it; his co-host couldn't get a handle on it. Two widebody samples were sent and one arrived disbonded at the throat out of the box.

04 · The lab anchor

Johnkew Pickleball

The most measurement-driven voice in the set, and the trust anchor for this page. Note: Johnkew's clip carried a sponsor/affiliate code (JohnQ), so the numbers — not the enthusiasm — are what we lean on.

Power

Q Core controlled-firepower 0.419 — middle of the high-power category, between the Les Inferno and the B&B Loco. Power and pop well-balanced, "not overly jumpy."

Spin

Upper half of the high category. Easy to shape the ball.

Specs

Swing weight 115; twist weight just over 6 — "a touch below average but holds up well for how light it is." Room to add weight.

Feel

Stiff-hollow quadrant but softened by the Elastek rubber layer — reminiscent of the 11.6 Power 2 series, slightly softer. Sweet spot comparable to a Gen 4 Loco.

Warranty caveat

6-month warranty, which Johnkew wished were a full year like most full-foam rivals; offset by a 99-day return policy.

A genuinely impressive, well-engineered full-foam paddle that delivers high power, excellent spin, a large sweet spot, and surprisingly pleasant feel at a strong-value price — a great fit for aggressive players who don't want to fully sacrifice control.— Johnkew Pickleball

05 · The independent voices

Non-sponsored reviews

Three of the 16 reviews carried no sponsorship or giveaway. They get the most weight here.

  1. Pickleball Studio (Chris & Isaac), full reviewThe Pro became "one of my favorite paddles" and a potential tournament qualifier if it gets UPA approval. Mid-to-upper power — strong but below the Boomstick, Q2, and Black Opal. Crisp, excellent directional control, "some of the best drives I've hit in a long time." Top choice now a toss-up between the Aura Pro and the Q2. Main knock: no durable grit.
  2. West Side Pickleball, vs. the J6CRThe Pro "completely blew me away." Beats the J6CR on power "hands down, and it's not close," elite-tier power after weight, stays consistent and controllable. Caveat: low dwell, stiff-and-hollow like the Loco, so hard to shape shots — and West Side personally prefers spin from dwell, so it's not his favorite-feeling paddle for singles despite winning on power.
  3. West Side Pickleball, vs. the B&B LocoThe Pro narrowly edges the Loco on power, control, spin, and dwell; "some of the nastiest topspin I've ever seen." Tied for his new main alongside the Carbon Barrage 1. (His later long-term Aura/Pro review was sponsored — see below.)
Where the independents convergeThe Pro is a real power paddle with elite-feeling spin and a big sweet spot, and it punches above $169. The recurring asterisks — non-durable grit and a stiff-hollow, low-dwell feel that limits shot-shaping — show up in the unpaid reviews too, which is the strongest signal they're real.

07 · Bottom line

Who should buy which

  1. Buy the Aura Pro ifYou're an aggressive, ~4.0+ player who wants big serves, hard drives, and elite spin, and you're willing to add a few grams of tape for stability. Best foam power paddle several reviewers had hit, at a fair price.
  2. Buy the Aura ifYou want all-court control, more dwell for shaping shots, the biggest forgiveness, and the lowest price (~$119–129). The most-recommended of the two by reviewers who ranked them, and the safer pick for doubles or improving players.
  3. Skip ifYou demand durable grit that won't fade (look at Spartus P1 / Vapor Power 2), you want maximum out-of-box stability with zero tinkering, or you're below ~3.0–3.5 and eyeing the Pro — get the Aura instead.
  4. Honest readStrong, well-engineered foam paddles that genuinely beat Friday's reputation, with two real caveats (stock stability, non-durable grit) that surfaced even in the unpaid reviews. The value is real; the hype is partly paid for.
